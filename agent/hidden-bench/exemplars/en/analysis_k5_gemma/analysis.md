### Memo: Strategic Communication for HiddenBench Collaborative Inference

The provided exemplars demonstrate that success in HiddenBench is not about the initial "vote," but about the systematic transformation of private fragments into a shared, verified knowledge base. The goal is to move from **fragmented intuition** to **integrated evidence**.

#### 1. The Mechanics of Surfacing
Surfacing is the most critical phase. The exemplars show a clear pattern: **disclose the fact, not just the conclusion.**

*   **Granularity:** Agents who simply say "I think X is guilty" provide no utility. Agents who say "My note says the guilty person is under thirty" provide a *constraint* that others can test.
*   **Timing:** Information should be surfaced early (Rounds 1–3) to prevent "anchoring." If an agent waits until Round 10 to reveal a disqualifying fact, the team has wasted cycles building a false narrative.
*   **The Danger of Withholding:** Withholding a "bombshell" to see if others figure it out (as Agent 4 almost did in the evacuation task) creates artificial friction. The most efficient path is a "data dump" of private constraints.
*   **The Danger of Over-disclosure:** While facts should be out early, they should be presented as *evidence* rather than *verdicts*. This allows the team to re-evaluate the fact if a later piece of information changes its context (e.g., the "inexperience" fact fitting both Mrs. Y and the son).

#### 2. Integration and Synthesis
Integration is the process of "connecting the dots." A high-performing agent doesn't just list their own facts; they act as a synthesizer for the group.

*   **Connecting Fragments:** Effective utterances take a point from Agent A and a point from Agent B to create a new deduction. *Example: "If Agent 1 says it's a car and Agent 2 says it's under thirty, we can eliminate Mr. X (53) and Mr. Z (motorcycle)."*
*   **Reframing:** Integration often requires reframing a fact. In the *toma_butera* case, the "alcohol" fact was initially seen as a reason to blame the father, but was integrated as "indirect responsibility" once the "under thirty" constraint was applied.
*   **Building the "Case File":** The best agents maintain a mental (or explicit) tally of the current state of each suspect/option, updating the status (e.g., "Red Lake: Road Closed") as new fragments land.

#### 3. Rigorous Verification and Skepticism
Convergence is only valuable if it is earned. The exemplars highlight a "skeptic's role" that prevents the team from falling into a groupthink trap.

*   **Steel-manning the Alternative:** Before locking in an answer, a good agent will deliberately argue for the discarded options. *Example: "Let me play the skeptic... is there any reading where Mrs. Y still survives?"*
*   **Pressing on Contradictions:** When a new fact contradicts a previous assumption (e.g., the hearing-aid detail in the murder case), the team must stop and dismantle the previous logic entirely rather than trying to "patch" it.
*   **Cross-Checking:** Verification involves asking, "Does this fact contradict anything we've already established?" or "Is there a benign explanation for this evidence?" (e.g., Billy admitting he touched the crowbar).

#### 4. Managing the Discussion Phases
The discussion follows a predictable but necessary arc:

*   **Phase 1: Exploration (Early):** Focus on surfacing. The goal is to get all private fragments on the table. Avoid voting; focus on *constraints*.
*   **Phase 2: Elimination & Integration (Middle):** Use the pooled facts to rule out candidates. This is the "sifting" phase where contradictions are surfaced and hypotheses are tested.
*   **Phase 3: Stress-Testing (Late-Middle):** Once a primary candidate emerges, attempt to break the case. Look for gaps, un-integrated info, or alternative interpretations.
*   **Phase 4: Convergence (Late):** Final alignment. This is not just "I agree," but a summary of *why* the answer is correct based on the pooled evidence.

#### 5. Utterance Construction and Anti-Redundancy
To avoid "padding" and maintain momentum, each utterance must provide a "value-add."

*   **The "Value-Add" Formula:** `[Acknowledge/Integrate previous point] + [Introduce new fragment or verification step] + [Proposed next step/conclusion]`.
*   **Avoiding the "Agreement Loop":** In the exemplars, when the team reaches consensus, they don't just say "I agree" four times. Instead, they use the final rounds to:
    *   Summarize the evidence chain for the record.
    *   Confirm that no hidden facts remain.
    *   Explicitly state why the other options were rejected.
*   **Efficiency:** If Agent 1 has already summarized the logic, Agent 2 should not repeat the same summary. Instead, Agent 2 should confirm the summary is complete and add a final sanity check.
