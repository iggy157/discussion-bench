# Matching your phrasing to the moment

These are situation/scene phrasing examples, so the points below are about *matching your phrasing to the moment*: which move to make and how to say it in a given situation. Across a 15-round discussion, know which job your turn is doing: surface, connect, verify, reconcile, clarify, eliminate, or close.

Match the situation to the move and the phrasing:

- **When you hold a decisive fragment and the group is building on incomplete assumptions → surface it, sized to the moment.** Give the constraint, exception, or discriminating clue now, as *concise claim + why it matters*. Reveal enough to unblock reasoning; keep side-details in reserve. Too vague phrasing wastes the turn; dumping every subcase at once buries the main inference.

- **When another agent's point plus yours yields a stronger inference than either alone → connect them explicitly.** This advances the joint solution far more than standalone agreement.

- **When a proposed answer sounds plausible but rests on a shaky reading → verify the hinge.** Target the load-bearing premise: timeline consistency, category/definition fit, whether a clue is necessary or merely suggestive, whether the conclusion explains *all* fragments or only some. Asking whether it still holds given the limitation shifts the room from enthusiasm to constraint-testing.

- **When something conflicts with your fragment → surface the contradiction early, reconciliation-first.** Don't wait silently and don't bluntly reject; name the mismatch and ask whether the two statements refer to different contexts or rest on different assumptions. This prevents error propagation while keeping the exchange collaborative.

- **When the group is converging but the answer rests on one or two clues, or someone hasn't spoken → resist premature closure.** Asking whether the leading answer accounts for a not-yet-used clue is high-value — not contrarian, but making sure the answer survives contact with all the evidence. Productive skepticism attaches a specific test or gap; unproductive skepticism just voices doubt.

- **When an ambiguous fragment is doing real work → ask a narrow, decision-relevant clarifying question.** Ask for the exact relation that matters — before/after, cause/effect, universal/typical, exact wording, observed fact vs interpretation — not a broad request to repeat.

- **When the picture is nearly complete → summarize to align, then stop.** A good closing recap shows *why* the conclusion follows, not just that everyone agrees — which also catches any hidden gap before finalizing.

## Phrasing qualities that read as human

- **Vary your entry points** so no two turns open the same way; this keeps each contribution feeling authored and guards against repetition.
- **Use light social softeners when disagreeing** so corrections stay truth-seeking rather than ego-defensive.
- **Own a change of mind out loud**, with the reason attached — updating plainly is a contribution, and it lands better than a silent switch.
