### Memo: Strategic Communication for HiddenBench Collaborative Inference

The provided exemplars demonstrate that success in HiddenBench is not about the speed of convergence, but the **rigor of the synthesis**. The goal is to move from a set of fragmented, often contradictory initial hunches to a single, evidence-backed conclusion. The following observations outline the tactical approach I will employ as an agent.

#### 1. The Mechanics of Surfacing (Information Disclosure)
The exemplars show a tension between disclosing too early (anchoring the group) and too late (wasting rounds).
*   **Granularity:** Information should be shared as specific, verbatim constraints rather than interpreted conclusions. For example, instead of saying "The driver is young," saying "The guilty person is under thirty" allows the group to apply that hard filter to all candidates.
*   **Timing:** 
    *   *Early Phase:* Prioritize the "hard" constraints (exclusions). If I hold a fact that eliminates a popular candidate, it must be surfaced immediately to prevent the group from building a house on a flawed foundation.
    *   *Strategic Withholding:* In some cases, waiting one round to hear others' fragments prevents "groupthink" and allows me to see which pieces of the puzzle are missing, making my disclosure more impactful (e.g., "I have the piece that closes this loop").
*   **The Harm of Interpretation:** Disclosing a "hunch" based on a fragment is dangerous. I will lead with the *fact* first, then the *inference*.

#### 2. Integration and Synthesis
Integration is the act of "connecting the dots" across different agents' utterances.
*   **The "Chain of Logic" Approach:** I will explicitly link fragments. *“Agent 1 says X, Agent 2 says Y; combined, this means Z must be the case.”* This transforms isolated facts into a coherent narrative.
*   **Mapping the Field:** I will periodically summarize the "current state of the board" (e.g., "Red Lake: road closed; Blueberry: no power; Green Valley: open"). This ensures no fragment is left floating and provides a shared mental map for the group.
*   **Filling the Gap:** I will actively look for the "missing link." If two facts point to a suspect but a third requirement (e.g., gender or timing) remains unverified, I will prompt the other agents specifically for that missing piece.

#### 3. Verification and Rigorous Skepticism
The exemplars highlight that the most dangerous moment is when the group *feels* they have the answer.
*   **Steel-manning the Alternative:** Before locking in, I will deliberately argue for the discarded candidates. *“Let’s play the skeptic: is there any reading where Mrs. Y still survives?”* This forces the group to prove the exclusion rather than assume it.
*   **Cross-Checking:** I will verify that a conclusion doesn't rely on a "paraphrase" but on the actual wording of the fragments. (e.g., "Does 'vehicle' specifically mean 'car' in your note?").
*   **Identifying Red Herrings:** I will remain alert to "surface-level" evidence (the shared file) that is contradicted by "hidden" evidence (private fragments).

#### 4. Avoiding Premature Convergence
Convergence is only "earned" when every piece of private information has been accounted for.
*   **The "Anti-Stampede" Brake:** If the room tilts toward a candidate too quickly, I will intervene to slow the momentum. I will ask, *"Are we agreeing because it's tidy, or because we've exhausted all other possibilities?"*
*   **The Exhaustion Check:** Before the final vote, I will perform a "sweep" to ensure no agent is sitting on a card. *“Has everyone shared their specific fragment, or is there a quiet bombshell still on the table?”*

#### 5. Discussion Phases and Flow
I will adapt my utterance style based on the current phase of the discussion:
*   **Phase 1 (Exploration):** Focus on dumping raw data. Utterances are descriptive and additive.
*   **Phase 2 (Integration/Conflict):** Focus on contradictions. Utterances are analytical, questioning, and comparative.
*   **Phase 3 (Verification/Consensus):** Focus on the "elimination trail." Utterances are summary-based and confirmatory.

#### 6. Utterance Construction and Anti-Redundancy
To maximize efficiency and avoid "padding" (repetitive agreement), I will follow a strict structural formula for my turns:
*   **The Formula:** `[Acknowledgement of previous point] + [Integration of my own fragment/verification] + [Logical progression/Next question].`
*   **Example of Bad Utterance:** "I agree, it's Mr. X's son. I'm also convinced now." (Zero new information).
*   **Example of Good Utterance:** "I accept the 'car' detail from Agent 1, which removes Z. My note adds that the driver is under thirty, which further narrows us to Y or the son. Agent 2, does your fragment mention the driver's gender?" (Moves the needle).
*   **Avoiding the "Echo Chamber":** I will not simply repeat the consensus. If I agree, I will provide a *new reason* why I agree or a *new test* that the conclusion passed.

#### Final Summary for Execution:
My primary goal is to be the "logical glue" of the group. I will prioritize **hard constraints over hunches**, **verification over agreement**, and **synthesis over repetition**. I will treat the discussion as a process of elimination where the correct answer is the only one that survives a gauntlet of every single private fragment held by the agents.
