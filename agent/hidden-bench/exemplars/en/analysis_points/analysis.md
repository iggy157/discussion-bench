# How a good 15-round discussion progresses

Here are the key points for running a good 15-round HiddenBench discussion. Make full use of all 15 rounds — develop the discussion step by step rather than rushing to a conclusion, and never restate a point already made.

A good discussion tends to move through this arc: surface (disclose) -> connect -> eliminate -> stress-test -> audit -> finalize. Concretely, aim for roughly this progression.

SURFACE (disclose) — roughly rounds 1-3
- Round 1: Open with each person's current lean, but treat all initial votes as shallow until private pieces are exposed.
- Round 2: Put hidden information on the table as discrete facts; reframe the task from "pick a favorite" to "pool non-overlapping constraints."
- Round 3: Compare the early fragments for fit and let first-anchor confidence soften — check whether an apparently strong clue survives the newly surfaced details.

CONNECT — roughly rounds 4-5
- Round 4: Start linking separate private facts into combinations, turning isolated clues into candidate profiles or scenario structures.
- Round 5: Reinterpret earlier-misread details in light of one another; move from several plausible stories toward one emerging synthesis.

ELIMINATE — roughly rounds 6-8
- Round 6: Use the pooled clues to rule options out directly — show that a candidate fails a hard constraint once facts are combined.
- Round 7: Revisit an early favorite against the new joint picture; separate superficial fit from fit across all dimensions.
- Round 8: Walk through and discard the residual alternatives; check whether any candidate can still stand without ignoring a revealed condition.

STRESS-TEST (verify) — roughly rounds 9-11
- Round 9: Ask what still speaks for the losing options, keeping dissent substantive rather than procedural.
- Round 10: Assemble a coherent end-to-end account for the leading answer — not just elimination of rivals but positive explanatory fit.
- Round 11: State where each person now stands and why they moved, so convergence is reasoned revision, not drift toward the majority.

AUDIT (take stock) — roughly rounds 12-14
- Round 12: Check explicitly whether the surviving case is strong enough under the task standard, noting the kind of evidence and any residual uncertainty.
- Round 13: Do a clue-by-clue accounting — verify that every surfaced private fact has been incorporated rather than left dangling.
- Round 14: Run final sanity checks from fresh angles — re-read the exact decision criterion, probe hidden assumptions, confirm agreement rests on articulated reasons.

FINALIZE (lock in) — round 15
- Round 15: Give a clean lock-in of consensus with a brief confirmation that no unresolved objection remains and the answer reflects the fully audited picture.

## Qualities that keep a 15-round discussion alive

How people talk matters as much as what they conclude. Aim for these:

- **Vary your openings and phrasing.** Don't begin turns the same way twice and never paraphrase-repeat a point already made; the variety is the simplest antidote to a repetitive, padded transcript.
- **Take turns playing skeptic.** Someone should keep testing the leading answer — ask what single fact would overturn it, have the surviving options walked through explicitly, and resist treating early agreement as rigor. This questioning is what fills the middle and late rounds instead of premature agreement.
- **Push back before you yield.** Resist on the merits first, then concede to the evidence, not to the crowd. The give-and-take makes the reasoning feel earned.
- **Own your bias out loud.** Say plainly when you change your mind and why; updating with the reason attached is a contribution, not a concession.
- **Name the progress.** Light metacognition — noting why no opening vote should be trusted alone, or that everyone has moved off their first pick — marks each phase and gives every round a reason to exist.
- **Keep a consistent individual stance.** Speak as a distinct participant across all 15 rounds so the discussion reads like several people rather than one voice, even at the brief close.
