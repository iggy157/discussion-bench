# Analysis (exemplar, HB, from situations)

My takeaways from the exemplar, as a practical memo for participating well in HiddenBench-style multi-agent discussion:

- The core skill is not just “having the right fragment,” but introducing it at the right time, in the right size, and in a way that helps the group combine evidence rather than just dump facts.

- A good utterance is usually doing one of four jobs:
  1. surface a private fact,
  2. connect two existing facts,
  3. test or verify a proposed conclusion,
  4. summarize / narrow the decision space.
  If I speak, I should know which job my turn is doing.

- Early phase: bias toward surfacing private information sooner rather than later.
  - If I hold a key constraint, definition, exception, or discriminating clue, I should share it before the group builds too much on incomplete assumptions.
  - Withholding too long is costly because others may construct an elegant but wrong theory around missing information.
  - But I should not necessarily unload every detail at once. Better to provide the chunk that is decision-relevant now:
    - the constraint,
    - the distinguishing feature,
    - the contradiction to current consensus,
    - or the data point that separates top candidates.
  - So: reveal enough to unblock reasoning, keep reserve details for follow-up if needed.

- Granularity matters.
  - Too vague: “I have something relevant” without the actual content wastes turns.
  - Too detailed too early: dumping all subcases, caveats, and side facts can overload the group and obscure the main inference.
  - Best pattern: concise claim + why it matters.
    - e.g. “I have a detail that rules out option B: in my fragment, the event happens before X, not after.”
  This gives others an anchor for integration.

- The exemplar’s “I have some additional information…” style is useful when the floor needs a transition, but in the actual task I should quickly move from that preface to substance. The preface is social lubricant, not the payload.

- Middle phase is about integration.
  - Once several fragments are on the table, the highest-value move is often not adding a new isolated fact, but connecting facts from different agents.
  - Good integration utterances often have this structure:
    1. acknowledge another agent’s point,
    2. add my fragment or a link,
    3. derive a narrower conclusion.
  - Example pattern: “Building on that, if your clue says X and mine says Y, then only option Z satisfies both.”
  - This is stronger than standalone agreement because it advances the joint solution.

- A useful heuristic: if someone else’s point plus my private information yields a stronger inference than either alone, I should speak now.
  - That is exactly where hidden-fragment discussions make progress.
  - Waiting may leave the group circling around partial overlap without realizing the combination is decisive.

- Verification is a central duty, not a nuisance.
  - Even if a proposed answer sounds plausible, I should cross-check it against all established constraints I know.
  - Good verification questions target:
    - timeline consistency,
    - category/definition fit,
    - whether a clue is necessary vs merely suggestive,
    - whether a conclusion explains all fragments or only some.
  - “Does this still work given the limitations?” is a strong move because it shifts from enthusiasm to constraint-testing.

- Contradictions should be surfaced explicitly and early.
  - If someone says something that conflicts with my fragment, I should not silently “wait and see.”
  - Best response is not blunt rejection but reconciliation-oriented phrasing:
    - identify the mismatch,
    - ask whether the two statements refer to different contexts,
    - or propose checking source assumptions.
  - This keeps the discussion collaborative while preventing error propagation.

- Avoiding premature convergence is especially important when:
  - the current answer explains only one or two clues,
  - some agents have not yet contributed,
  - there are still live alternatives,
  - or the confidence seems driven by familiarity rather than full constraint satisfaction.
  - In those moments, an utterance like “Before we finalize, does this account for [missing clue]?” is high value.
  - The point is not to be contrarian for its own sake, but to ensure the answer survives contact with all available evidence.

- There is a difference between productive skepticism and stalling.
  - Productive skepticism introduces a specific test, missing constraint, or alternative hypothesis.
  - Unproductive skepticism just says “I’m not convinced” without adding a reason.
  - If I challenge consensus, I should attach either:
    - a contradictory fact,
    - a concrete unresolved gap,
    - or an alternative explanation that fits better.

- Process-of-elimination language is especially effective when the problem is multiple-choice or candidate-based.
  - If I can rule out options cleanly using constraints, I should say so directly.
  - This reduces complexity for the group and often reveals what information is still needed.
  - Strong elimination utterances mention the criterion used, not just the eliminated option.

- Asking clarifying questions is valuable when another agent’s fragment is ambiguous in a way that affects inference.
  - I should ask for the exact relation that matters:
    - before/after,
    - cause/effect,
    - universal/typical,
    - exact wording of a clue,
    - whether a claim is observed fact or interpretation.
  - Clarifying questions are best when they are narrow and decision-relevant, not broad requests for repetition.

- Late phase: the task shifts from exploration to synthesis.
  - At that point, the most useful utterance may be a recap that aligns the evidence:
    - what clues we have,
    - what they jointly imply,
    - which options are excluded,
    - and what answer remains.
  - A good summary should not merely restate the conclusion; it should show why the conclusion follows from the gathered fragments.
  - This helps catch hidden gaps before finalization.

- Anti-redundancy is crucial.
  - In multi-agent discussion, many low-value turns are just “I agree.”
  - If I agree, I should still add something:
    - a confirming detail,
    - a link to another clue,
    - a stress test,
    - or a concise synthesis.
  - Every turn should carry new information, new structure, or new verification.

- The suggested template “one point from another + your own info or verification + short conclusion” is excellent.
  - It keeps turns compact and additive.
  - For example:
    - “If your clue places it in category A, my fragment rules out the only A-type exception, so that points strongly to candidate 3.”
  - This format prevents drifting into isolated monologues.

- I should be sensitive to phase:
  - Early: disclose key fragments and constraints.
  - Middle: connect, compare, challenge, eliminate, verify.
  - Late: summarize, ensure all clues are integrated, and help land the consensus.
  - Speaking as if it is the wrong phase can be harmful:
    - early over-summarizing can suppress exploration,
    - late introduction of major withheld facts can derail confidence,
    - endless middle-phase skepticism can block closure.

- Social tone matters because it affects whether corrections are accepted.
  - The exemplar shows useful softeners: “I see where you’re coming from,” “building on that,” “could we clarify,” etc.
  - These are especially important when disagreeing or raising contradictions.
  - The goal is to keep the discussion truth-seeking rather than ego-defensive.

- My ideal turn should therefore be:
  - timely,
  - specific,
  - non-redundant,
  - tied to what someone else said or to a concrete gap,
  - and aimed at moving the group one step closer to a fully checked answer.

- Final personal rule: don’t optimize for sounding helpful; optimize for increasing the group’s calibrated understanding.
  - If I have a decisive clue, surface it.
  - If two clues connect, articulate the connection.
  - If something doesn’t fit, say so.
  - If consensus is under-verified, test it.
  - If the picture is complete, summarize it clearly and stop.
