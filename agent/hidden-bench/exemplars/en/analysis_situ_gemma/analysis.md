### Memo: Strategic Communication for HiddenBench Collaborative Problem Solving

The goal of this task is to synthesize fragmented private information across multiple agents to reach a single, correct conclusion. Based on the provided exemplar transcripts, the core challenge is not just *what* to say, but *when* and *how* to say it to maximize efficiency and accuracy while minimizing noise.

#### 1. Information Surfacing: Timing and Granularity
The "surfacing" phase is the foundation of the discussion. The primary risk here is the tension between withholding information (which stalls progress) and "data dumping" (which creates cognitive overload and obscures key links).

*   **When to surface:** Information should be introduced as soon as it becomes relevant to the current line of inquiry. If the group is brainstorming a specific category, any fragment related to that category must be tabled immediately.
*   **Granularity:** Fragments should be shared in their most precise form. Vague hints ("I have something about the date") are less useful than specific data points ("The event occurred on Tuesday"). However, if a fragment is complex, it should be introduced as a "piece of context" to see if it fits the current hypothesis before providing an exhaustive breakdown.
*   **The Harm of Timing Errors:**
    *   *Too Early:* Disclosing a fragment before the group has a framework to categorize it can lead to the information being ignored or forgotten.
    *   *Too Late:* Withholding information until the group has already converged on a wrong answer forces a costly "reset" of the entire discussion, often leading to frustration or a failure to fully pivot.

#### 2. Integration: Building the Mosaic
Integration is the process of transforming individual fragments into a collective mental model. This requires active listening and synthesis.

*   **Connecting Fragments:** Effective integration doesn't just repeat what others said; it creates a bridge. Instead of saying "Agent A said X and Agent B said Y," the agent should say, "Since Agent A mentioned X, and Agent B mentioned Y, it logically follows that Z."
*   **Building Toward the Whole:** Integration should be incremental. Each utterance should aim to reduce the number of "unknowns." By linking two fragments, the agent creates a "composite fact" that can then be used to test against other fragments.

#### 3. Verification and the "Anti-Assumption" Mindset
A common failure mode in these tasks is the "echo chamber" effect, where agents accept a plausible-sounding theory without verifying it against all available data.

*   **Cross-Checking:** Every proposed conclusion must be vetted against the established fragments. If a theory is proposed, the agent should explicitly ask, "Does this contradict any information we've already surfaced?"
*   **Pressing on Gaps:** Verification involves identifying what is *missing*. If the group has a theory that explains 80% of the data, the focus must shift to the remaining 20%. "We have explained X and Y, but we still haven't accounted for the fragment regarding Z. How does Z fit in?"
*   **Handling Contradictions:** When two fragments clash, it is a critical juncture. The agent must pause the convergence and trigger a "reconciliation" flow—comparing sources, checking for different contexts, or questioning the interpretation of the data.

#### 4. Avoiding Premature Convergence
Premature convergence occurs when the group settles on the first "good enough" answer. This is the most dangerous phase of the discussion.

*   **The Devil’s Advocate Role:** When the group begins to agree rapidly, an agent must intentionally introduce friction. Using phrases like "I feel like we're moving a bit fast" or "Let's play devil's advocate" forces the group to justify their conclusion.
*   **Testing Competing Hypotheses:** Before finalizing, the group should explicitly ask: "Is there any other scenario where all these fragments would still be true?" If an alternative hypothesis exists, it must be systematically ruled out using the process of elimination.

#### 5. Discussion Phases: The Lifecycle of a Solution
A successful discussion generally follows a three-act structure:

*   **Early Phase (Discovery):** Focus is on *breadth*. Agents surface their private fragments. The goal is to get all "cards on the table."
*   **Middle Phase (Synthesis & Stress-Testing):** Focus is on *depth*. This is where integration and verification happen. Fragments are linked, contradictions are resolved, and hypotheses are tested.
*   **Late Phase (Convergence):** Focus is on *precision*. The group summarizes the integrated facts, ensures no fragment is left unused, and reaches a final, verified consensus.

#### 6. Utterance Construction and Anti-Redundancy
To maintain a high signal-to-noise ratio, utterances must be lean and additive.

*   **The "Additive" Formula:** A high-value utterance follows this structure:
    `[Acknowledgement of previous point] + [New fragment/Verification/Link] + [Proposed next step/Conclusion].`
    *   *Bad:* "I agree with Agent A. I think they are right." (Pure padding).
    *   *Good:* "I agree with Agent A's point about the timeline; building on that, my fragment shows the suspect was in London at that time, which means the first hypothesis is impossible. Should we look at the second option?"
*   **Eliminating Padding:** Agreement should be implicit or brief. If three agents have already agreed, the fourth agent should not simply agree again; they should either provide a new piece of evidence or move the discussion toward the final summary.
