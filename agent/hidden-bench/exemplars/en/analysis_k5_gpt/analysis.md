# Analysis (exemplar, HB, from scripts_k5)

Key pattern across these exemplars: good discussion is not “argue your initial vote harder,” but “convert private clues into shared constraints, then actively test whether the emerging story satisfies all constraints at once.” The best utterance depends on phase.

- Early phase: surface your private fragment clearly and concretely.
  - If your clue is discriminative, say it early in a form others can use as a constraint, not just a conclusion.
    - Good: “My note says the guilty person was driving a car.”
    - Worse: “I think Z is wrong.”
  - Include exact wording when wording matters. In several transcripts, resolution hinged on a phrase like “car,” “man,” “under thirty,” “tourists only,” or “execution is on hold.” Precision prevents drift.
  - Distinguish observation from inference.
    - “The owner tested at 1.5 alcohol” is better than “Mr. X is guilty.”
    - “C’s main kitchen is out, but cold menu runs on backup” is better than “C is probably okay.”
  - Don’t withhold a decisive blocker too long. The best discussions quickly eliminate impossible options once a strong hidden fact appears (sinkhole, norovirus, bank freeze).
  - But don’t front-load speculative interpretation if the room hasn’t pooled facts yet. Early over-arguing from a single clue can anchor the team badly.

- Granularity: give enough detail to be actionable, but not a speech.
  - Usually the useful unit is: exact private fact + brief implication.
    - “No one witnessed Eddie working that morning, so his alibi is self-reported.”
    - “Skylake’s co-investor condition was satisfied yesterday.”
  - If your clue has an important caveat, include it immediately. Many traps are “apparent disqualifier, but with carve-out”:
    - Green Valley closed to tourists, not evacuees.
    - C’s main kitchen out, but cold prep still works.
    - Crowbar prints explained by admitted innocent contact.
  - Omitting the caveat can mislead the group into false elimination.

- Middle phase: integrate, don’t merely restate.
  - The strongest utterances combine someone else’s fact with yours or with a shared fact to reduce the candidate set.
    - “If it must be a car, Z is out.”
    - “Under thirty plus male plus father indirectly responsible leaves only the son.”
    - “Camera until 6:45 plus wrong-direction geography eliminates Mickey despite motive.”
  - A useful template is:
    1. pick up one established fact,
    2. connect it to another fact,
    3. state the narrowed implication.
  - This is how fragmented evidence becomes a coherent case rather than a pile of clues.

- Verification: challenge gently but concretely.
  - Good discussions do not accept a pivotal claim without checking its wording, scope, or applicability.
    - “Is ‘car’ exact wording or your paraphrase?”
    - “Frozen, or merely slowed?”
    - “Does the angel group meet Skylake’s own requirement for this deal size?”
  - This kind of verification is especially important when:
    - a fact overturns multiple initial votes,
    - a detail seems too convenient,
    - an inference stretches beyond the stated fact.
  - Press on role distinctions and hidden assumptions.
    - Owner vs driver.
    - Tourist closure vs emergency use.
    - Main kitchen outage vs total shutdown.
    - Speed in theory vs ability to execute in practice.
  - Verification is not obstruction. It protects against solving the wrong problem too neatly.

- Avoiding premature convergence:
  - When the room starts to align, that is exactly when to ask: what still points the other way?
  - Exemplars repeatedly do “steelman the losing candidate once more.”
    - “Could Mrs. Y still survive?”
    - “What’s the best remaining case for Billy?”
    - “Could Peak still clear fastest?”
  - This serves two functions:
    1. catches overlooked hidden info,
    2. makes the final consensus evidence-based rather than social.
  - Also explicitly ask if anyone still holds an unspoken private fact. HiddenBench rewards complete pooling; false consensus often comes from unshared fragments.

- Phased behavior:
  - Early:
    - Contribute your private fact.
    - Encourage full pooling.
    - Avoid overcommitting to your pre-answer.
  - Middle:
    - Cross-check facts against each other.
    - Eliminate options with hard constraints.
    - Resolve contradictions, caveats, and timing issues.
    - Ask targeted questions to clarify exact wording or applicability.
  - Late:
    - Summarize the elimination chain cleanly.
    - State why alternatives fail, not just why the winner looks good.
    - Help convert tentative convergence into explicit consensus.
    - Surface any residual doubt before lock-in.

- “Which situation calls for which utterance”:
  - If you hold a hard exclusion no one else can infer: say it immediately.
    - Example types: route closed, contamination, regulatory freeze, exact demographic constraint.
  - If the room is anchored on a candidate your fact weakens: introduce your fact as a correction, not a confrontation.
    - “That changes how I read X…”
  - If others have surfaced complementary clues: make an integrating utterance.
    - “Putting A and B together, only C fits.”
  - If the discussion is moving too fast: ask a verification question.
    - “Is that exact wording?”
    - “Does your fact exclude all use, or only normal use?”
  - If consensus is forming but a rival hasn’t been tested: steelman the rival.
    - “Before we close, what is the strongest remaining case for Y?”
  - If everyone is repeating agreement: instead of “I agree,” add a concise synthesis or unresolved check.
    - “Agreed, and the reason is newer information overrides the morning report.”
    - “Agreed, but let’s confirm no one has a negative access fact about Green Valley.”

- Anti-redundancy:
  - Weak late-round behavior is pure padding: “same here,” “I’m convinced too,” repeated over many turns.
  - Strong late-round behavior adds one of:
    - a compact elimination summary,
    - a clarification of causal chain,
    - a check for hidden dissent,
    - explicit reversal of prior belief with reason,
    - operational implications if relevant.
  - Even if you agree, your utterance should move the group:
    - close a loophole,
    - reconcile two clues,
    - retire a concern,
    - verify no missing facts remain.

- Good discourse norms seen repeatedly:
  - Admit when your initial vote was wrong.
  - Reward evidence over ego.
  - Be explicit when your own fact only partially narrows the field.
    - “My note alone can’t separate Y from the son.”
  - Note when another agent’s fact is decisive against your own prior position.
  - Treat newer, more specific, or more direct facts as overriding older shared assumptions.

- Practical speaking template for HiddenBench:
  - “My piece is X, stated precisely.”
  - “That means / does not necessarily mean Y.”
  - “Combined with Agent N’s point about Z, it rules out / strongly supports Q.”
  - “Before we lock that, can we verify [one gap]?”
  - “If that check passes, I’m ready to move to Q.”

Bottom line: the right utterance is phase-sensitive and information-efficient. Early, contribute hidden constraints. Middle, integrate and interrogate. Late, summarize and test residual alternatives. The best contribution is rarely a bare vote; it is usually one compact move that either adds a missing fact, fuses two facts into a consequence, or checks a load-bearing assumption before consensus hardens.
