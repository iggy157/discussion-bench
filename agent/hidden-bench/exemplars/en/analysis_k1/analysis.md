# Observed flow of the reference discussions

In the reference examples, the discussions generally follow this arc: surface (disclose) -> connect -> eliminate -> stress-test -> audit -> finalize. Concretely, they tend to unfold as follows.

SURFACE (disclose) — roughly rounds 1-3
- Round 1: The discussions tend to open with competing intuitions and a shared recognition that initial votes are shallow until private pieces are exposed.
- Round 2: Hidden information tends to enter as discrete fragments here, and the group reframes the task from "pick a favorite" toward "pool non-overlapping constraints."
- Round 3: Early fragments tend to be compared for fit, with first-anchor confidence softening as the group tests whether apparently strong clues survive contact with newly surfaced details.

CONNECT — roughly rounds 4-5
- Round 4: The group tends to start linking separate private facts into combinations, turning isolated clues into candidate profiles or scenario structures.
- Round 5: Previously misread details tend to be reinterpreted in relation to one another, and the discussion moves from several plausible stories toward one emerging synthesis.

ELIMINATE — roughly rounds 6-8
- Round 6: The pooled clues tend to start ruling options out directly, usually by showing that a candidate fails a hard constraint once facts are combined.
- Round 7: The group tends to revisit an early favorite in light of the new joint picture, separating superficial fit from fit across all dimensions.
- Round 8: Residual alternatives tend to be walked through and discarded, with the group checking whether any candidate can still stand without ignoring a revealed condition.

STRESS-TEST (verify) — roughly rounds 9-11
- Round 9: The discussions tend to pause and ask what still speaks for the losing options, keeping dissent substantive rather than merely procedural.
- Round 10: A coherent end-to-end account tends to be assembled for the leading answer, showing not just elimination of rivals but positive explanatory fit.
- Round 11: Participants tend to state where they now stand and why they moved, so convergence reads as reasoned revision rather than drift toward the majority.

AUDIT (take stock) — roughly rounds 12-14
- Round 12: The group tends to check explicitly whether the surviving case is strong enough under the task standard, noting the kind of evidence and any residual uncertainty.
- Round 13: A clue-by-clue accounting tends to appear here, with participants verifying that every surfaced private fact has been incorporated rather than left dangling.
- Round 14: The discussions tend to stay substantive with final sanity checks from fresh angles — re-reading the decision criterion, probing hidden assumptions, and confirming that agreement rests on articulated reasons.

FINALIZE (lock in) — round 15
- Round 15: The ending tends to be a clean lock-in of consensus, with a brief confirmation that no unresolved objection remains and that the answer reflects the fully audited picture.

This is how the reference scripts use all 15 rounds — the arc above is what fills them.

## What makes these scripts readable and human

Beyond the round-by-round arc, the reference scripts stay engaging because of *how* the agents talk, not just *what* they conclude. These qualities are worth carrying over — they are what turn 15 rounds into a discussion worth reading rather than a padded march to a foregone answer:

- **Varied, in-character openings.** Agents rarely start a turn the same way twice. The variety of entry points makes each turn feel authored rather than templated, and it is the simplest antidote to repetition.

- **A facilitator/skeptic voice that keeps the room honest.** One agent tends to take on a testing role. This questioning is what naturally fills the middle and late rounds instead of premature agreement.

- **Genuine pushback before yielding.** Agents do not fold instantly; they resist on the merits first and then concede to the evidence, not to the crowd. The give-and-take is what makes the reasoning feel earned.

- **Owning one's own bias out loud.** Changing your mind with the reason attached reads as human and gives the eventual convergence real weight.

- **Light metacognition — talking about the discussion itself.** Naming the progress marks each phase and gives every round a reason to exist.

- **Distinct but consistent personas.** Each agent keeps a recognizable stance across all 15 rounds — the one holding a pivotal fact who refuses to over-claim, the steady facilitator who tests before agreeing — so the transcript reads like four different people rather than one voice split four ways, even at the close.

Carry these qualities over — varied phrasing, a skeptic who stress-tests the leading answer, real pushback, honest changes of mind, and consistent individual voices — so the discussion is both substantive and enjoyable to read all the way to Round 15.
