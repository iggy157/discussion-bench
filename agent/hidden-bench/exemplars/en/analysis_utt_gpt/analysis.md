# Analysis (exemplar, HB, from utterances_k5)

A few strong patterns stand out across these exemplar slices. The big meta-lesson is that good HiddenBench discussion is not “state your guess louder,” but “turn private fragments into a shared constraint system.” The best utterances do one of four jobs at the right time: surface a hidden fact, integrate multiple facts into a narrower hypothesis set, verify a weak link or possible contradiction, or close consensus only after checking nothing material is still unspoken.

- **Early phase: surface your private fragment promptly, but in usable form**
  - The strongest early utterances do not just announce a vote. They reveal the hidden evidence with enough specificity to be actionable:
    - exact wording if the wording matters (“driving a car” vs “vehicle”),
    - timing if chronology matters,
    - whether the fact is hard exclusion vs soft tendency.
  - Good granularity is usually: **fact + why it matters + what it seems to rule in/out**, without overclaiming certainty.
    - Example pattern: “My note says X. That cleanly rules out Y, but doesn’t yet distinguish A from B.”
  - That last clause is important. It prevents your fact from being oversold.
  - Harm of withholding too long:
    - The group keeps optimizing on incomplete data and anchors on wrong suspects/options.
    - Others may build elaborate reasoning around a hypothesis your fact would quickly kill.
  - Harm of disclosing too early in the wrong form:
    - If you lead only with a conclusion (“it’s Eddie”), others can anchor on your answer rather than the evidence.
    - If you dump an ambiguous fragment without caveats, the group may misuse it.
  - So the useful move is: **share your private card early, but as evidence, not just verdict**.

- **Integration phase: connect fragments, don’t merely stack them**
  - The best middle-game utterances often have this structure:
    1. restate one or two other agents’ key facts,
    2. connect them to your own or to the shared file,
    3. show what combination follows.
  - This is more valuable than saying “I agree.”
  - Typical integration moves:
    - convert separate clues into eliminations:
      - “car” rules out motorcycle,
      - “under thirty” rules out the father,
      - “man” rules out Mrs. Y.
    - convert one agent’s exculpatory fact into another suspect’s inculpatory fact:
      - Billy’s touching the crowbar explains the prints, while Eddie’s impossible hearing claim looks fabricated.
    - reframe apparent negatives as compatible when paired:
      - private booking + secondary room open,
      - power outage + cold menu functioning,
      - tourist closure + emergency shelter still open.
  - A key observation: many discussions hinged on **facts that looked anti-candidate in isolation but became pro-candidate when combined with another fact**. So a useful utterance asks: “Does this detail actually conflict, or does another agent’s detail explain it?”
  - This also means good participants explicitly mark uncertainty:
    - “Alone, my fact pointed me to Y, but with Agent 1’s car detail it has to refer to the son instead.”

- **Verification: pressure-test the hinge, not random details**
  - Strong discussions repeatedly stop before locking in and ask for verification on the decisive point.
  - Common good verification moves:
    - ask for exact wording when ambiguity matters (“car” specifically?),
    - check recency/conflict of evidence (“new alert overrides morning inspection?”),
    - test whether a condition is truly satisfied (“finalized partnership, or just in talks?”),
    - distinguish owner from driver / motive from opportunity / closure for tourists from closure for evacuees.
  - This is not generic skepticism. It is targeted at the “load-bearing” premise.
  - Best practice: when another agent’s fragment is doing major work, invite them to restate it precisely.
    - That both strengthens reliability and reduces hidden paraphrase drift.
  - Good verification also includes self-correction:
    - “My original theory rested on X; if X collapses, my case evaporates.”
  - That kind of utterance helps the group update cleanly instead of defending sunk costs.

- **Avoiding premature convergence: explicitly test survivors and alternatives**
  - The exemplars repeatedly avoid “everyone now says the same name, so done.”
  - Better pattern:
    - narrow to two candidates/options,
    - ask whether the losing option can still survive all pooled facts,
    - identify any hidden note not yet spoken,
    - only then converge.
  - Useful anti-premature-convergence utterances include:
    - “Let me play skeptic once more.”
    - “What’s the strongest remaining objection?”
    - “Do we have any unvoiced fact still sitting in someone’s hand?”
    - “By my count this still leaves A and B; what breaks the tie?”
  - This is especially important when:
    - multiple candidates fit your fragment,
    - the group is changing answers quickly,
    - the answer currently rests by elimination alone.
  - A good discussion doesn’t fear elimination, but it prefers **elimination backed by explicit constraints** rather than social momentum.

- **Late phase: consensus-building should summarize the full evidence map**
  - Once the major contradictions are resolved, useful late utterances do one of two things:
    - concise synthesis of all critical facts,
    - explicit around-the-room check for dissent or missing info.
  - The most persuasive late summaries are compact and structural:
    - “Car rules out Z, under thirty rules out X, male rules out Y.”
    - “Peak is frozen, Northstar is three weeks out, Skylake’s condition is already satisfied.”
  - This helps ensure the final answer is not just unanimous but justified.
  - A late “I agree too” with no new content is mostly padding unless the task rewards explicit alignment. Even then, better to add:
    - what changed your mind,
    - which fact was decisive,
    - or one final consistency check.

- **Utterance construction: compact, additive, non-redundant**
  - Best utterances usually contain:
    - **one point from another speaker**,
    - **one contribution of your own** (new fact, clarification, or inference),
    - **a short conclusion**.
  - Example template:
    - “If Agent 2’s timing is right, then my note about the route kills Mickey. That leaves Billy and Eddie, and Billy’s prints are explained. So I’m moving to Eddie.”
  - This keeps the discussion moving.
  - Anti-patterns:
    - repeating the current consensus without adding anything,
    - restating all facts every turn,
    - defending your initial vote after your own premises have failed,
    - hiding behind vagueness (“I have something that might matter”) for too long.
  - Another good pattern is **role separation**:
    - one speaker surfaces,
    - another integrates,
    - another verifies the hinge,
    - another summarizes and checks consensus.
  - If everyone only summarizes, the discussion stalls; if everyone only introduces new facts without integrating, the picture stays fragmented.

- **Practical rule of thumb for my own participation**
  - If early and my fact is decisive or strongly constraining: state it clearly now.
  - If another person’s fact plus mine creates a stronger inference than either alone: make that connection explicit.
  - If the room is converging on a conclusion that depends on a shaky interpretation: ask a precise verification question.
  - If we seem nearly done: summarize the constraint chain and ask for any unvoiced dissent or hidden fragment.
  - If my initial guess is weakened: say so plainly and explain why. Updating is a contribution.

Overall, the “right” utterance depends on phase:
- **Early:** evidence exposure.
- **Middle:** linkage and pressure-testing.
- **Late:** synthesis and disciplined closure.

The core discipline is to make every turn advance the collective state: reveal, narrow, test, or lock. Not just echo.
