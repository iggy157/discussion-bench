# HiddenBench Discussion missing_lab_sample

## Task
The team must decide where the missing experimental virus vial most likely is — Cold Storage B, Lab 2 Bench, or the Decontamination Room — so containment can begin without wasting time on the wrong location.

## Pre-answers
- **Agent 1**: {"vote": "Cold Storage B", "rationale": "The technician normally stores samples there and the sensor logged a brief opening as she finished, though I know that sensor has been unreliable."}
- **Agent 2**: {"vote": "Decontamination Room", "rationale": "Cleaning logs show an odd after-midnight entry, so the vial may have been swept up despite the room looking undisturbed."}
- **Agent 3**: {"vote": "Cold Storage B", "rationale": "It's her default location and the room is the obvious place she'd return to, even if I saw nothing left when I locked up."}
- **Agent 4**: {"vote": "Lab 2 Bench", "rationale": "She was interrupted by an urgent call right after her task and rushed toward Lab 2, which could mean the vial went with her."}

## Round 1
- **Agent 1**: Let's lay out what we actually know before guessing. The shared picture points hard at Cold Storage B: it's her habitual drop-off spot, and the temperature sensor caught it being opened right when she wrapped up.
- **Agent 2**: That's the surface read, sure. But I'd be careful about treating "undisturbed" rooms as cleared. My piece of information complicates the Decontamination Room, so I don't want us to write it off too fast.
- **Agent 3**: I'm inclined toward B as well, but inclination isn't evidence. Before we anchor, let's each say what specific detail we were handed that the others might not have.
- **Agent 4**: Agreed, and I'll go first because mine cuts against the consensus. The part I was given is that the technician got an urgent phone call the moment she finished — and a witness saw her moving fast toward Lab 2.

## Round 2
- **Agent 1**: That's worth pausing on. The shared log says Lab 2 has no entry or removal record for the sample in the last two days. People keep reading that as "it was never there," but an unlogged, rushed hand-off wouldn't show up in a log at all.
- **Agent 2**: Right. A clean log only proves nobody followed procedure, not that the vial is absent. If she bolted mid-task, procedure is exactly what gets skipped.
- **Agent 3**: Fair point. Still, the sensor data on B is concrete — it opened when she finished. That's a physical signal, not a habit assumption.
- **Agent 4**: It would be, if the sensor were trustworthy. Agent 1, you hinted you know something about that reading.

## Round 3
- **Agent 1**: I do, and I should have led with it. The part I was given: maintenance reports the door sensor on Cold Storage B has been malfunctioning all week, throwing false open/close readings constantly. So that "brief opening" may be noise.
- **Agent 2**: That meaningfully weakens the strongest pillar under the Cold Storage B theory. Without the sensor, all we have left for B is "she usually puts things there."
- **Agent 3**: And habit is exactly the kind of thing that breaks when someone is interrupted. Okay — this is shifting for me, but I have a fact that bears directly on B too.
- **Agent 4**: Go ahead. Let's get everything on the table before anyone commits.

## Round 4
- **Agent 3**: The detail I hold: another researcher was working late in Cold Storage B last night and locked up after midnight. He says there were no unlogged vials left in the room when he secured it.
- **Agent 1**: That's close to decisive for ruling B out. A faulty sensor plus a human eyewitness who saw nothing extra — the "she stored it there" story has very little holding it up now.
- **Agent 2**: It also means the sensor opening, real or false, didn't correspond to a vial being deposited. Good. So where does that push us?
- **Agent 4**: It pushes us toward the two places her interrupted exit could have led: she carried it toward Lab 2, or it ended up in Decontamination somehow. Agent 2, your room is still open.

## Round 5
- **Agent 2**: Here's my fact. The Decontamination Room's cleaning log, read closely, shows a cleaning that happened unexpectedly after midnight — but the supervisor annotated it as a routine wipe-down for dust only, nothing involving samples.
- **Agent 1**: So the "undisturbed this morning" note in the shared brief fits: the off-hours cleaning was minor and already done, not a sample event.
- **Agent 3**: That actually argues against a vial being in there. If a real biohazard had turned up, a dust wipe-down wouldn't be how it got logged.
- **Agent 4**: And nobody reported finding or disposing of a vial during that cleaning. An unexpected wipe is curious, but it's not a sample sighting.

## Round 6
- **Agent 2**: I'll concede that. I came in leaning Decontamination because the after-midnight entry looked suspicious, but once it's just dust and the room reads undisturbed, there's no actual link to the vial.
- **Agent 1**: I appreciate you putting the log detail out there rather than just dropping your vote — it let us close the question properly instead of guessing.
- **Agent 3**: So two of our three locations are now thinly supported. B has a bad sensor and a witness against it; Decontamination has only an unrelated cleaning note.
- **Agent 4**: Which leaves Lab 2, the one place tied to an actual observed action — her rushing there with a sample in hand, mid-interruption.

## Round 7
- **Agent 1**: Let me steelman the Lab 2 objection, because I don't want us to swing from one anchor to another. The strongest counter is the log: no record of the sample in Lab 2 for two days.
- **Agent 2**: But we already addressed that. The log reflects procedure, and procedure is the first casualty of an urgent phone call. A vial set down on a bench in a hurry never gets entered.
- **Agent 3**: There's also a subtle point: the log shows neither entry nor removal. If the vial were properly in Lab 2's system, you'd expect at least an entry. Its total absence is consistent with an undocumented, rushed drop.
- **Agent 4**: Exactly my read. The witness saw direction and speed — toward Lab 2, quickly. That's the only positive evidence of where the vial physically went.

## Round 8
- **Agent 1**: Let me test the witness chain. Agent 4, do we know she was still holding the vial when she headed to Lab 2, or just that she went there?
- **Agent 4**: The account I was given is that the call came "just after finishing her task," and she moved rapidly toward Lab 2. It doesn't itemize her hands. So I'll be honest: it's strong but not airtight.
- **Agent 2**: That honesty matters. But pair it with Agent 3's eyewitness in B — no vial left there — and the vial clearly didn't stay in storage. If it left B with her and she went to Lab 2, the chain closes.
- **Agent 3**: And she "claimed to have finalized her drop-off." If she believed she finished but was interrupted, she might genuinely think it's in B while it's actually sitting on a Lab 2 bench.

## Round 9
- **Agent 1**: That reframing is persuasive. Her belief that the task was complete explains why she returned the key confidently — and why the vial isn't where she thinks it is.
- **Agent 2**: So the false "I finished" and the empty B converge on the same explanation: interrupted hand-off, sample diverted to Lab 2.
- **Agent 3**: I want to be rigorous though. Is there any scenario where the vial is still in B despite the eyewitness? Maybe it was logged elsewhere and physically in B?
- **Agent 4**: If it were in B, the late-working researcher securing the room after midnight would most likely have seen an out-of-place vial. He specifically reports none. I think B is genuinely out.

## Round 10
- **Agent 1**: I'm now comfortable moving off my original Cold Storage B vote. The two things that drew me there — habit and the sensor — are both undermined, one by malfunction and one by a witness.
- **Agent 2**: Same trajectory for me, away from Decontamination. My own log detail turned out to point at dust, not danger.
- **Agent 3**: I'll shift too, but let me name the residual risk so we're not just agreeing: if we're wrong about Lab 2, we burn time there while a hazardous vial sits unsecured. That's the cost.
- **Agent 4**: A real cost. So let's weigh it explicitly rather than assume. Where does the probability mass actually sit now?

## Round 11
- **Agent 1**: For Cold Storage B: faulty sensor (no reliable opening signal) plus a direct eyewitness of an empty room. I'd put that low.
- **Agent 2**: For Decontamination: an after-midnight cleaning logged only as dust, room found undisturbed, no vial reported. Also low.
- **Agent 3**: For Lab 2 Bench: a witnessed rapid movement there during an interrupted task, a "completed" claim that doesn't match reality, and a log absence that fits an undocumented drop. That's the highest, even if no single fact is a smoking gun.
- **Agent 4**: So the ranking is Lab 2 clearly first, with B and Decontamination trailing. We didn't get here by following the loudest early vote — we got here by pooling what each of us held.

## Round 12
- **Agent 1**: One more check before we lock in: did anyone's hidden detail actively support a location, rather than just weaken one? Because most of our evidence has been eliminative.
- **Agent 4**: Mine is the affirmative one — the sighting heading to Lab 2. That's the single piece pointing somewhere rather than away from somewhere.
- **Agent 2**: Right, and eliminative evidence plus one strong affirmative pointer is a solid combination. The eliminations clear B and Decontamination; the sighting names Lab 2.
- **Agent 3**: I'm satisfied. The reasoning isn't "majority picks Lab 2," it's that every competing location has a concrete fact against it and Lab 2 has a concrete fact for it.

## Round 13
- **Agent 1**: Operationally, I'd still keep a fast secondary sweep of Cold Storage B queued, given the stakes — but Lab 2 Bench is where the first team goes.
- **Agent 2**: Agreed on prioritization. Search Lab 2 benches immediately, focusing on wherever an interrupted person would set something down quickly near the entrance.
- **Agent 3**: And contact the technician directly: ask whether she set the vial down in Lab 2 when the call came. She may resolve it in one sentence.
