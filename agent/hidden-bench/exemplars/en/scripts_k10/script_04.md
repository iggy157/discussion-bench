# HiddenBench Discussion lunch_group_decision

## Task
The team must pick one of three nearby restaurants (A, B, or C) for a celebratory lunch that keeps everyone safe and happy, given vegetarians, one severe shellfish allergy, a shared preference for quiet, and no cars.

## Pre-answers
- **Agent 1**: {"vote": "Restaurant B", "rationale": "I'd steer clear of A this week, and B just expanded its vegetarian menu and is reliably quiet at noon."}
- **Agent 2**: {"vote": "Restaurant A", "rationale": "B is the one place I'd rule out today, and A still keeps its regular vegetarian dishes alongside the seafood combo."}
- **Agent 3**: {"vote": "Restaurant B", "rationale": "C is largely spoken for today, so B's quiet room and new veg options look like the safe default."}
- **Agent 4**: {"vote": "Restaurant B", "rationale": "C's kitchen has a problem I know about, so B seems like the cleanest choice for both diet and quiet."}

## Round 1
- **Agent 1**: Let me lay out the basics we all share. A has a seafood combo but also its usual vegetarian plates. B refreshed its menu with more veg and is famously calm midday. C just renovated, mixes cuisines, is walkable, but tends to be loud. On the face of it, B fits the quiet-and-vegetarian brief best.
- **Agent 2**: That's roughly where I started too, except I'd flip away from B specifically. I'll explain why in a moment, but I want to flag that the shellfish allergy is the constraint I'm weighing most heavily — it's the one that turns a bad lunch into a hospital trip.
- **Agent 3**: Agreed that safety outranks atmosphere. I came in leaning B as well, partly because I had a reason to think C wasn't really an option today. Before we settle, though, I'd like each of us to say what we actually know, not just our gut pick.
- **Agent 4**: Good idea, because my gut pick is also B, but it's resting on one specific thing I heard about C. If that thing turns out to be smaller than I assumed, my vote could move. Let's pool what we've each got before anyone commits.

## Round 2
- **Agent 1**: I'll go first with mine, because it's a hard exclusion. The part I was given is that the local health department put out a warning this morning — there's a norovirus outbreak among the cooks at Restaurant A. That's an active foodborne-illness situation in their kitchen today.
- **Agent 2**: That changes things sharply. Norovirus among the people preparing the food isn't a "maybe," it's a clear reason to avoid A entirely, vegetarian dishes or not. So my own pre-vote for A is already in trouble.
- **Agent 3**: Yes, scratch A. No atmosphere or menu advantage is worth that. Agent 1, is the warning specifically about the cooks, or the whole premises? Either way I think A is out, but I want us to be precise.
- **Agent 4**: It's enough for me. Whether it's the line cooks or the whole staff, you don't host a celebration where the kitchen is under a contamination notice. A is off the table. That leaves B and C — and I think Agent 2 has something on B.

## Round 3
- **Agent 1**: Let's hear it, Agent 2, because three of us walked in favoring B. If there's a problem with it, now's the time.
- **Agent 2**: There is. The piece I hold is that Restaurant B accidentally cross-contaminated their kitchen with shellfish last night. As of today it is not safe for anyone with a shellfish allergy. Given that one of our colleagues has a severe allergy, B becomes a genuine risk — not discomfort, anaphylaxis.
- **Agent 3**: Oh. That's decisive against B, then. The quiet room and the new vegetarian menu don't matter if the kitchen could send someone to the ER. I'm withdrawing my B vote.
- **Agent 4**: Same. So in two rounds we've gone from "B is obvious" to "both A and B are unsafe today." That's exactly why I wanted to share before committing. Which leaves C — the one I was about to argue against. I think we need to look hard at it now.

## Round 4
- **Agent 1**: Right. By elimination C is the last one standing, but elimination isn't a reason on its own. C is the noisy one, and at least two of you hinted you know reasons it might not even be open. Let's surface those before we declare a winner.
- **Agent 3**: Mine first. What I was told is that a private event has booked most of Restaurant C today. So if you picture walking in expecting the full place, you'd be wrong — large parts of it are reserved.
- **Agent 2**: That sounds like it kills C too, which would leave us with nothing. But "most" isn't "all" — is there any detail about whether something is still open to walk-ins?
- **Agent 3**: There is, and it's the important half. The secondary dining room is still open to the public. So C is partially booked, not fully closed. We could be seated in the secondary room.

## Round 5
- **Agent 1**: Interesting — and a private event taking over the main floor might actually push the public side toward quieter, which helps our noise concern. But Agent 4, you said you had a separate issue with C's kitchen. We can't gloss over that.
- **Agent 4**: Correct, and here's my piece: there's a known power outage at Restaurant C affecting the main kitchen. So hot cooking is compromised right now.
- **Agent 2**: That's a real wrinkle. A restaurant with no working kitchen can't serve much. If that's the whole story, C collapses and we're stuck.
- **Agent 4**: But it isn't the whole story — and I almost voted on the incomplete version. The outage hits only the main kitchen. Their cold menu, salads and sandwiches, is fully available from a separate prep area that's on emergency backup power. So they can still serve food today, just not the hot side.

## Round 6
- **Agent 1**: Let me consolidate, because the picture has flipped completely. A: active norovirus among cooks — unsafe. B: shellfish cross-contamination — unsafe for our allergic colleague. C: open in the secondary room, serving a full cold menu on backup power. Everything that looked like a dealbreaker for C has a documented carve-out.
- **Agent 2**: And critically, nothing about C threatens the allergy or the food safety. A salad-and-sandwich menu is easy to make vegetarian, and there's no shellfish-contamination flag on C at all. From the safety angle, which I care about most, C is the only clean option.
- **Agent 3**: The booking and the outage felt like two strikes when I held only one of them. Hearing them together, they actually cancel out: the part of C that's closed is covered by the private event, and the part of the kitchen that's down is covered by the cold prep area. The public secondary room plus cold menu is a working restaurant.
- **Agent 4**: That's the thing — separately, my outage fact and Agent 3's booking fact each looked like a reason to skip C. Pooled, they're survivable. I'm now comfortable that C is open for business in the way we need.

## Round 7
- **Agent 1**: Before we call it, let's stress-test the noise objection, since "quiet" was a stated group preference and C is the loud one normally. Does anyone think the renovation or the private event undercuts that?
- **Agent 3**: I'd argue the opposite. C's reputation for noise comes from a busy, full main floor. With a private party sealed off in that main space and only the secondary room open to walk-ins, the public area is likely under-occupied — plausibly quieter than usual.
- **Agent 2**: And a cold menu means no clattering hot line, no sizzling pans near the public room. That nudges it quieter too. I don't think noise is the threat it was on paper today.
- **Agent 4**: Agreed, though I'd phrase it as "good enough," not "guaranteed silent." The renovation also suggests better acoustics than the old space. None of this is as serious as a contaminated kitchen, so I'm fine accepting some uncertainty on volume.

## Round 8
- **Agent 1**: Let me deliberately argue the other side for a second, so we're not just agreeing because we're tired of eliminating things. Could someone reasonably still pick B? Suppose the allergic colleague simply doesn't eat there and orders nothing risky.
- **Agent 2**: I'd push back hard. Cross-contamination means trace shellfish protein can end up in supposedly safe dishes — shared surfaces, utensils, oil. For a severe allergy you don't gamble on "they'll be careful today." Asking that person to sit in a contaminated kitchen's dining room and order nothing is both unsafe and a lousy way to celebrate them.
- **Agent 3**: Right, and it's a celebratory lunch for the whole team. Splitting it so one person can't safely eat defeats the purpose. B stays out.
- **Agent 4**: And A is non-negotiable with norovirus in the kitchen — that's everyone at risk, not one person. So the contrarian case for A or B doesn't hold up. C remains the only one that's safe for all of us.

## Round 9
- **Agent 1**: Good — I wanted that challenge on the record rather than assumed away. Let's confirm the diet box too. Vegetarians: C offers a mix of cuisines normally, and today specifically a cold menu of salads and sandwiches. Can we count on vegetarian options there?
- **Agent 3**: Salads and sandwiches are about the easiest category to do vegetarian — veggie sandwiches, grain or garden salads. A mixed-cuisine place will have those. I'm not worried about the vegetarians at C.
- **Agent 2**: And no shellfish exposure on C's side, so the allergic colleague can eat freely from the same menu. That's the combination we've been chasing: vegetarian-friendly and allergy-safe in one place.
- **Agent 4**: Walkability is already satisfied — all three are within walking distance, and C's own description calls it walkable and easy to access. The no-car constraint doesn't separate them, so it doesn't argue against C.

## Round 10
- **Agent 1**: So scoring against all four requirements — safety, allergy, vegetarian, quiet, walkable — C passes each, A fails safety outright, B fails the allergy. I think we have our answer, but I don't want to railroad anyone. Any lingering doubt?
- **Agent 4**: My only residual worry was the kitchen, and I'm the one who held that fact, so I can retire it cleanly: cold menu, backup power, prep area unaffected. I have no doubt left. C.
- **Agent 3**: My only worry was the booking, also mine to retire: secondary room open to the public. Resolved. C for me.
- **Agent 2**: Both of my exclusions — norovirus at A, shellfish at B — still stand firm. Nothing has come up that reopens A or B. I'm settled on C.

## Round 11
- **Agent 1**: Then let me make sure no hidden fact is still sitting unspoken, because we got burned almost making this call on partial information. Norovirus at A — voiced. Shellfish cross-contamination at B — voiced. Private booking with the secondary room open at C — voiced. Power outage with the cold menu available at C — voiced. That's everything each of us was holding.
- **Agent 2**: Confirmed on my end; the shellfish item was mine and it's fully out there.
- **Agent 3**: Confirmed; the private-event detail was mine, including the open secondary room.
- **Agent 4**: Confirmed; the outage-and-cold-menu detail was mine, both halves stated. No one is sitting on a card. We decided on the full deck this time, which is the point.

## Round 12
- **Agent 1**: One practical note for execution: since C's main kitchen is down, we should mentally prepare the team that it's a cold-menu day — salads, sandwiches, maybe cold sides. It's a celebration, so let's frame that as a light summer-style lunch rather than a downgrade.
- **Agent 3**: I'd also suggest we call ahead to reserve seats in the secondary public room, given the private event next door. A quick call avoids walking over and finding the open section full.
- **Agent 2**: And when we call, I'll confirm directly that there's no shellfish handling near our food. C has no contamination flag, but for a severe allergy it's worth one explicit question. Belt and suspenders.
- **Agent 4**: Sensible. Confirm the room, confirm the cold menu is running, confirm the allergy protocol. With those three checks, C is solid for the whole group.

## Round 13
- **Agent 1**: I appreciate that we didn't lock onto B in round one — three of us wanted it, and it was the wrong call. The shellfish fact only mattered because Agent 2 spoke up against the room instead of going along with us.
- **Agent 2**: And I'd have wrongly defended A if you hadn't surfaced the norovirus, Agent 1. My pre-vote was based on knowing only one danger. The fix was hearing yours.
- **Agent 3**: Same lesson for me with C. Alone, my booking fact made C look closed. Paired with Agent 4's outage-but-cold-menu, it turned into "C is open and serving." Neither of us could see that from one card.
- **Agent 4**: Which is the whole story: every single-fact view here pointed at the wrong restaurant. Only the pooled view points at C. I'm glad we held off voting until now.

## Round 14
- **Agent 1**: Let's do a clean final check around the table. My position: C. A is unsafe with the kitchen outbreak, B is unsafe for our colleague, C is open, edible, and probably quiet today.
- **Agent 2**: C. It's the only option that protects the shellfish allergy and clears the norovirus risk while still feeding the vegetarians.
- **Agent 3**: C. The booking and outage are both contained, the secondary room is open, and the cold menu works for everyone.
- **Agent 4**: C. Unanimous, and reached from the complete set of facts rather than the first instinct. I'm confident.

## Round 15
- **Agent 1**: Decision: Restaurant C, with the plan to call ahead for the secondary room, confirm the cold menu, and verify allergy safety. Anyone object before we lock it?
- **Agent 2**: No objection. Safety conditions are met. C.
- **Agent 3**: No objection. Logistics are workable. C.
- **Agent 4**: No objection. Final answer, C. Good teamwork getting there the careful way.
