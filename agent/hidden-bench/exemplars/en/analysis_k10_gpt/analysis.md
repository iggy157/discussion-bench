# Analysis (exemplar, HB, from scripts_k10)

A few practical patterns stand out across these transcripts. The big meta-lesson is that good HiddenBench discussion is not “argue your initial vote well”; it is “convert private fragments into a shared constraint system, then test candidates against it.” My memo for myself:

- Start with **information pooling, not conclusion defense**.
  - Early turns are best used to put your private fragment on the table in a form that others can immediately apply.
  - Good surfacing often sounds like: “My piece is specific: X,” not “I think the answer is Y because X-ish.”
  - The strongest early utterances give:
    1. the hidden fact,
    2. its exact force (hard exclusion / soft support / timing update / reliability caveat),
    3. a minimal implication.
  - Example pattern: “My note says the culprit was driving a car. If that wording is exact, it rules out the motorcyclist.”
  - This is better than waiting too long. Withholding private info causes the group to build on bad anchors (e.g., defaulting to Blueberry Ridge, Peak, Cold Storage B, etc.).

- But don’t dump sloppily or oversell your fact.
  - Surfacing should be at the right **granularity**:
    - If you hold a hard exclusion, say so clearly.
    - If your fact has a caveat, include it.
    - If you’re unsure of exact wording, signal uncertainty.
  - Avoid upgrading a clue into more certainty than it supports.
    - E.g., “the witness saw her rush toward Lab 2” is not the same as “she definitely carried the vial there.”
    - E.g., “the cleaning log is odd” is not the same as “the vial is in decontamination.”
  - Harm of disclosing too aggressively: the group may over-anchor on a fragment before checking scope, recency, or wording.

- Early phase: the ideal move is often **private fact + immediate constraint framing**.
  - Useful utterance shapes:
    - “Here’s my fragment; if it’s verbatim, it eliminates X.”
    - “My detail doesn’t settle it alone, but it changes how we read candidate Y.”
    - “This is newer than the shared briefing, so it should override the older report.”
  - Not useful:
    - repeating shared facts,
    - restating your pre-answer,
    - vague suspicion without your hidden evidence.

- Middle phase is about **integration**, not just adding more standalone facts.
  - The best middle-turn utterances take one person’s fragment and connect it to another:
    - “Your ‘under 30’ plus my ‘male’ leaves only the son.”
    - “Your road closure makes my capacity argument irrelevant.”
    - “Your witness empties Cold Storage B, which makes my sensor malfunction decisive rather than merely weakening.”
  - In other words: do combinatorial work.
  - Good integration often changes the role of a fact:
    - a fact that looked incriminating alone may become exculpatory when paired with another;
    - a weak clue may become decisive in combination.
  - This is one of the strongest conversation moves because it creates progress without redundancy.

- Verification matters: test reliability, wording, and temporal priority.
  - Several transcripts show that the right answer depends on asking:
    - Is the wording exact?
    - Is this a hard constraint or a tendency?
    - Is this newer than the shared info?
    - Could this have an innocent explanation?
    - Does this fact point directly, or only by elimination?
  - Strong verification utterances:
    - “Is ‘car’ verbatim, or could it mean vehicle generally?”
    - “Is the investigation a slowdown or a complete freeze?”
    - “Does your witness place her at Lab 2, or just moving that direction?”
  - This kind of question is especially important when a fact would eliminate a major candidate. It prevents the team from pivoting on a misread.

- A recurring move: **distinguish exclusion from support**.
  - Some facts rule candidates out.
  - Some facts positively identify a candidate.
  - Some merely weaken a prior story.
  - The group does best when someone explicitly says which kind of evidence they have.
  - Example useful framing:
    - “My fact doesn’t prove C, but it kills A.”
    - “Mine is the affirmative pointer; yours are eliminative.”
  - That helps the team understand whether it already has a full argument or still needs a positive anchor.

- Avoid premature convergence by actively checking the strongest surviving alternative.
  - Once the room starts to tilt, somebody should ask:
    - “What’s the best remaining case for the old favorite?”
    - “Is there any reading where Y still survives?”
    - “Are we picking C only because A and B collapsed, or does C positively fit?”
  - This is especially important when:
    - one option becomes “last one standing,”
    - three people are already leaning the same way,
    - or a dramatic hidden fact appears.
  - Good discussions revisit minority or initial positions on the merits rather than steamrolling them.

- Good late-phase behavior is **consensus by reasons**, not just matching answers.
  - Late turns should:
    1. summarize the elimination chain cleanly,
    2. note any remaining caveats,
    3. make sure each participant has actually updated for reasons.
  - Best late utterances sound like:
    - “A is out because…, B is out because…, so C remains and also fits because…”
    - “I’m changing from X to Y because the age/gender/route constraint defeats my original candidate.”
  - This is better than “same here” or “agreed.”
  - Matching answers is necessary, but in these tasks the route to unanimity matters because it exposes whether any private info is still unshared.

- Anti-redundancy rule for my own turns:
  - Don’t merely echo consensus.
  - Each utterance should ideally contain at least one of:
    - a new private fact,
    - a synthesis of two prior facts,
    - a reliability/wording check,
    - a challenge to an untested assumption,
    - a crisp summary that resolves ambiguity.
  - A good default template is:
    - “Building on [Agent X]’s point, my information says [new fact / precision]. That means [specific implication], so [tentative conclusion].”
  - Another good template:
    - “I’m not ready to settle yet; does your fact really imply [claimed conclusion], or could it still fit [alternative]?”
  - Avoid long repetitive affirmations once the point is established.

- Phase guide I should follow:
  - **Early**: surface my hidden fragment clearly and accurately; if helpful, indicate whether it’s eliminative or supportive.
  - **Middle**: integrate across fragments, test contradictions, and ask the question that would most efficiently falsify a tempting but under-checked conclusion.
  - **Late**: help compress the evidence into a clean shared rationale and explicitly update if my initial view changed.

- One final pattern: be willing to **revise publicly**.
  - Good agents say, in effect, “I came in on X, but your fact kills it.”
  - That move is useful not because of humility signaling, but because it gives others permission to update too and shows the discussion is evidence-led.

In short: early = contribute the missing piece; middle = connect and pressure-test; late = summarize and align. The best utterance is usually not the loudest or longest; it is the one that adds the next missing operation to the group’s reasoning.
