### Memo: Strategic Communication for HiddenBench Collaborative Deduction

The provided exemplar demonstrates a high-functioning collaborative deduction process. The core challenge of HiddenBench is not just the possession of fragmented data, but the management of *cognitive anchoring*—the tendency for agents to cling to their initial "pre-answer" based on incomplete information. To succeed, I must treat the discussion as a systematic filter, moving from a wide pool of suspects to a single, verified conclusion.

#### 1. Surfacing: The Art of Information Disclosure
The exemplar shows that the "Surfacing" phase is the foundation of the entire process. 

*   **Timing and Granularity:** Information should be disclosed early (Round 1) but with precision. Agent 1 didn't just say "the driver was drunk"; they specified "the *car owner* tested at 1.5." This distinction later became the hinge for the entire case.
*   **The Danger of Vague Disclosure:** If an agent summarizes their fragment too broadly (e.g., "My note says someone was speeding"), it can lead to false convergence. Granularity—providing the exact phrasing or specific constraints (e.g., "under thirty," "a man")—allows other agents to cross-reference their fragments accurately.
*   **The Harm of Withholding:** Withholding information to "test" others slows the process and increases the risk of premature convergence on a wrong answer. The goal is a collective pool of facts, not a game of riddles.

#### 2. Integration: Building the Composite Picture
Integration is the process of synthesizing disparate fragments into a coherent narrative.

*   **Connecting the Dots:** Integration happens when one agent picks up a constraint from another and applies it to the shared data. For example, when Agent 2 took Agent 1’s "car" constraint and used it to challenge Agent 3’s "110 km/h" (which originally pointed to the motorcyclist).
*   **Reframing:** A key integration technique is *reframing*. When the "drunk driver" theory failed the age test, the team reframed the "drunk owner" as the "indirectly responsible father." This allowed a previously contradictory fact to fit the emerging truth.
*   **The "Filter" Approach:** Integration should function like a sieve. 
    *   *Filter 1 (Vehicle Type) $\rightarrow$ Filter 2 (Age) $\rightarrow$ Filter 3 (Gender).*
    *   Each integrated fragment should explicitly eliminate candidates until only one remains.

#### 3. Verification and Avoiding Premature Convergence
The most critical failure mode in these tasks is "groupthink," where agents agree too quickly because a hypothesis *seems* plausible.

*   **The Role of the Skeptic:** Agent 4 serves as the "Verification Engine." Even when the team is leaning toward the son, Agent 4 asks, "Is there any reading where Mrs. Y still survives?" This forces the team to prove the exclusion of other candidates rather than just the inclusion of the favorite.
*   **Cross-Checking:** Verification requires checking the final hypothesis against *every* single fragment disclosed. If a theory explains the age and gender but ignores the alcohol level or the speed, it is incomplete.
*   **Pressing on Contradictions:** When Agent 3 hesitated to drop Mr. Z, the team didn't just tell them to agree; they asked for the *exact wording* of the "car" constraint. Verification is based on verbatim evidence, not interpretations.

#### 4. Discussion Phases and Flow
A successful discussion follows a predictable but rigorous trajectory:

*   **Phase 1: The Data Dump (Early):** All agents surface their private fragments. The focus is on transparency and precision.
*   **Phase 2: The Elimination Process (Middle):** The team applies the surfaced fragments to the suspect list. This is the "clash" phase where initial pre-answers are discarded in favor of pooled evidence.
*   **Phase 3: The Stress Test (Late-Middle):** The team attempts to break the leading hypothesis. They look for gaps or alternative explanations (e.g., "Could Mrs. Y fit this?").
*   **Phase 4: Convergence and Locking (Late):** Once the hypothesis is bulletproof, the team explicitly aligns. This is not just about saying "I agree," but summarizing *why* the answer is correct based on the pooled facts.

#### 5. Utterance Construction and Anti-Redundancy
To maintain efficiency and avoid "padding," each utterance must provide a "value-add."

*   **The "Value-Add" Formula:** 
    *   *Acknowledge/Integrate* (e.g., "If the car detail is verbatim...") $\rightarrow$ *Apply/Verify* (e.g., "...then Z is out, regardless of speed") $\rightarrow$ *Propose Next Step* (e.g., "Let's apply the age constraint next").
*   **Avoiding the "Agreement Loop":** In the exemplar, the agents avoid simply saying "I agree" for multiple turns. Instead, they use their agreement to reinforce a specific piece of evidence (e.g., Agent 2 explaining *why* the son fits "inexperience" better than Mrs. Y).
*   **Final Confirmation:** The final rounds should be a concise, unanimous lock-in to ensure no one is silently disagreeing.
