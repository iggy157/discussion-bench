# Analysis — talk (exemplar, from utterances_k5)

Based on the provided exemplar transcripts, here is my causal mapping and analysis of the game's discussion dynamics.

### 1. Board Situation $\rightarrow$ Agenda Mapping
The primary driver of the discussion is the "information state" of the board. The agenda shifts based on the following variables:

*   **Seer CO Distribution:**
    *   **1-CO (Single Claim):** The agenda is **Verification**. The village focuses on the Seer's reasoning for their target and whether the result (Human/Werewolf) aligns with the target's behavior.
    *   **2-CO+ (Counter-Claims):** The agenda shifts to **Resolution**. The priority is no longer the result itself, but the *identity* of the fake. The discussion focuses on "mirroring" (did the second claim just copy the first to muddy waters?) and "asymmetry" (who is welcoming a second night of testing vs. who is rushing a lynch?).
*   **The Result (Black vs. White):**
    *   **Werewolf Result:** High-tempo, aggressive push for a lynch. The agenda is **Execution**.
    *   **Human Result:** Lower tempo. The agenda is **Filtering**. The cleared player becomes a "trusted" pillar, and the focus shifts to the remaining suspects.
*   **Night-Attack Result:**
    *   **No Kill:** Suggests a "safe" night or a wolf holding back. Increases pressure on the Seer to provide a definitive lead.
    *   **Seer Killed:** Triggers a **Post-Mortem**. The village analyzes the Seer's final claims and looks for who fought hardest to discredit them (the "Wolf's Fingerprint").
*   **Player Count/Role Distribution:**
    *   As numbers dwindle (e.g., 3-4 players), the agenda shifts from "gathering vibes" to **Mathematical Necessity**. The discussion becomes more clinical, weighing the remaining possibilities.

### 2. Discussion Patterns & Situational Triggers
*   **The "Mirroring" Critique:** Arises specifically during 2-CO situations. A player argues that the second claimant is merely reacting to the first to appear helpful or to create a 50/50 gamble.
*   **The "Quiet Wolf" vs. "Loud Wolf" Debate:** Arises during Day 0 or early Day 1. This is a meta-discussion used to frame suspects—either accusing the silent players of hiding or the loud players of steering the narrative.
*   **The "Cover-Pair" Analysis:** Arises when two players consistently vouch for each other. The agenda is to identify a Wolf-Possessed or Wolf-Villager alliance.

### 3. Per-Day Phases
*   **Day 0 (The Setup):**
    *   *Focus:* Self-introductions, establishing "rules of engagement" (e.g., "let's claim early"), and light behavioral profiling.
    *   *Tone:* Cautious, polite, but observant.
*   **Day 1 (The Reveal):**
    *   *Focus:* Morning claims $\rightarrow$ Result sharing $\rightarrow$ Reasoning/Q&A $\rightarrow$ Vote.
    *   *Tone:* High tension. The shift from "vibes" to "data" happens here.
*   **Day 2+ (The Refinement):**
    *   *Focus:* Analyzing the night kill $\rightarrow$ Checking consistency of previous claims $\rightarrow$ Narrowing the suspect pool.
    *   *Tone:* Analytical and increasingly urgent.

### 4. Turn Progression & Closing
*   **Turns 0–3:** Deliberation, questioning, and building the case. This is where the "causal" work of the discussion happens.
*   **The Final Turn:** The "Closing." The deliberation ends abruptly. The final utterance must be a clear vote declaration using the `@name` format. No new arguments are introduced in the final turn; it is for execution only.

### 5. Spread of Responses (Avoiding Echo Chambers)
To maintain a natural, multi-agent feel, responses to a main agenda are distributed:
*   **The Affirmative:** Concise agreement ("Agreed," "Makes sense").
*   **The Nuanced:** Agreement with a caveat ("I agree, but we should also watch X").
*   **The Contrarian:** A different angle or a delayed judgment ("I'm not sold on that logic yet").
*   **The Observer:** Neutral tracking ("Noted, I'll watch how that plays out").
*   **The Passive:** Skipping the current topic to maintain a "quiet" persona.

### 6. Off-Mainstream Positioning (Handling Attack)
When a character is targeted, they avoid a simple "I am innocent" binary:
*   **The Logical Defender:** Admits the appearance of guilt but explains it through a villager's lens ("I was being cautious, not guilty").
*   **The Refocusing Organizer:** Acknowledges the attack but pivots the group back to a larger puzzle ("You can vote me, but that doesn't solve the Seer conflict").
*   **The Stoic:** Remains neutral, letting the evidence speak or challenging the attacker's motive.
*   **The Gambler:** Dares the village to make a mistake ("If you're wrong, the wolf wins. Think carefully").

### 7. Utterance Construction & Voice
The "Golden Thread" of a high-quality utterance follows a specific structural flow:
**[Reference to previous speaker/point] $\rightarrow$ [Injection of new judgment/logic] $\rightarrow$ [Short, punchy conclusion/call to action].**

*   *Example Logic:* "You say quiet is guilty (Reference), but a wolf can be loud to hide in the noise (Judgment). I'll be watching the pushers (Conclusion)."
*   *Consistency:* Voice is maintained through specific linguistic markers (e.g., one character prefers "math/logic," another prefers "vibes/tone," another prefers "structure/procedure").
