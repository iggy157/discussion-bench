# Constructing a strong single contribution

These are individual utterance examples, so the points below are about how to construct a strong single contribution — phrasing, granularity, and how to raise a point. Make every turn earn its place: claim, give a reason, question, or reposition — never just echo.

- **Make a claim or result land as evidence, not just an assertion.** State it plainly, then give the reason with enough specificity to act on — why this target, why this timing.
- **Connect to the thread, don't monologue.** A strong move picks up one prior speaker's point, adds your own evaluation or a causal rule, and ends with a small lean.
- **Question the load-bearing point, not everything.** Targeted verification — why that target, why claim second, why this order — is worth far more than generic doubt; attach a specific inconsistency or a who-benefits.
- **Keep contributions compact and additive.** One point picked up + one contribution of your own + a short conclusion. Don't restate the whole board every turn, and don't defend a stance your own reasoning has already undercut.

## Utterance qualities that read as human

- **Vary how you open.** Don't start two turns the same way; the variety keeps each line feeling authored rather than templated.
- **Push back before you yield.** Resist on the merits first, then concede to the evidence, not to the crowd — the give-and-take makes a read feel earned.
- **Own a wrong read out loud.** Conceding a changed mind with the reason attached reads more human than flawless certainty.
- **Use light social softeners when you disagree** to keep the exchange truth-seeking rather than ego-defensive.
