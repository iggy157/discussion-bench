# Analysis — talk (exemplar, from situations)

My main observation is that the “good” transcript style is less about clever standalone lines and more about matching the current board state to the right conversational job. The dialogue feels functional: each utterance advances the day’s immediate agenda, and the agenda itself changes predictably based on CO count, result pressure, deaths, and remaining headcount.

- **Board situation -> agenda**
  - **Day0 / no hard info yet**
    - Agenda is light process-setting, temperature-taking, and establishing readable tone.
    - Players do self-intros, mild positioning, maybe preferences about CO timing or voting standards.
    - Tempo is relaxed; strong accusations are premature unless someone is conspicuously strange.
    - The causal rule: when no one has privileged info on the table, the group values “future readability” more than solving immediately.
  - **Day1 with 1 seer CO**
    - Agenda shifts to whether to accept the lone CO provisionally, ask for timing/intent, and build a gray-list from reactions.
    - Tone is cautious but not explosive; a single CO does not end discussion, it redirects it toward support/pressure structure.
    - Key question becomes: who aligns too easily, who resists oddly, who asks useful verification questions?
    - If no black result exists, the day often stays broad; if a black result exists, the target and claimant immediately become central.
  - **Day1 with 2+ seer COs**
    - Agenda becomes comparison, contradiction hunting, and deciding whether to resolve among claimants or their result-targets.
    - Tempo speeds up because competing narratives create urgency.
    - Questions focus on consistency, incentives, and whether one CO’s behavior better fits a truthful role.
    - The more COs there are, the less room there is for abstract process talk; concrete sorting dominates.
  - **A black result appears**
    - This sharply narrows the day.
    - Agenda becomes: evaluate the black claim, hear from the accused, inspect the accuser’s prior behavior, and decide whether to treat the result as actionable now.
    - Tone gets firmer; even neutral players are expected to state a direction.
    - But good discussion still distinguishes “logically possible defense” from “most plausible world.”
  - **No attack / unusual night result**
    - Agenda expands into causality speculation: guard success, wolf misfire, deliberate no-attack, role-interaction implications.
    - This tends to invite overreach, so stronger transcripts often include someone grounding speculation in probability rather than pure possibility.
    - Tone can become more analytical and less accusatory for a stretch.
  - **Normal night attack on Day2+**
    - Agenda is heavily consistency-based: compare today’s surviving claimants and yesterday’s statements against the attack result.
    - Who benefitted from the kill? Whose story gained or lost plausibility?
    - The death acts as a new piece of alignment evidence, not just flavor.
  - **Few players remaining / endgame**
    - Agenda compresses: less broad exploration, more forced choice.
    - Tone becomes explicit and decisive; each utterance should help lock a world model or eliminate one.
    - The transcript style reserves the end for clean vote commitments rather than continued wandering.
  - **Revealed-role distribution shifts**
    - Whenever an executed or attacked player’s role becomes known, prior reads are recalibrated.
    - If a previously distrusted player flips human, pressure increases on those who pushed weakly.
    - If a wolf flips, associative reads matter more, but good discussion usually avoids overclaiming from one connection.

- **Discussion patterns and when they arise**
  - **Verification Q&A pattern**
    - Most common immediately after a CO or strong result.
    - Players ask for basis, sequence, and fit with earlier comments.
    - The function is not only to test the claimant, but to generate a reaction map from everyone else.
  - **Consistency-check pattern**
    - Strongest on Day2+ or after contradictory statements accumulate.
    - Someone points out tension between a current claim and an earlier posture, asking for reconciliation rather than jumping straight to condemnation.
    - This creates a measured tone: “explain this gap” first, then decide whether it is suspicious.
  - **Pre-vote organization pattern**
    - Tends to arise once enough material exists and the day is nearing close.
    - One speaker recaps options, asks if anything critical is missing, and channels the room toward explicit declarations.
    - This is especially important in multi-claimant situations where discussion can sprawl.

- **Per-Day phase structure**
  - **Day0**
    - Self-introductions, stylistic positioning, process agreement, light impressions.
    - Reads are soft; the goal is to seed future interpretation.
  - **Day1**
    - Morning info dump matters: COs, result-sharing if applicable, then verification and early sorting.
    - Midday becomes pressure-testing and tentative vote direction.
    - End of day should harden into clear vote declarations.
  - **Day2+**
    - Start with the attack result and what it changes.
    - Then compare present claims against prior statements and revealed roles.
    - More emphasis on consistency and less tolerance for vague hedging.

- **Turn progression**
  - **Turn 0-3 rough flow**
    - Early turns establish the day’s primary agenda.
    - Mid turns branch into support, challenge, caveat, or reframing.
    - Late turns collapse back toward decision.
  - **Final turn discipline**
    - Important observation: the last utterance should usually be the actual vote declaration with an @-mention.
    - Don’t waste the final slot on open-ended deliberation.
    - The transcript feels “good” when the close is operational, not conversational.

- **Spread of responses to one agenda**
  - Good scenes do not have everyone saying the same kind of thing.
  - On one central issue, responses distribute across roles:
    - concise agreement,
    - agreement plus a warning/caveat,
    - a different angle,
    - a neutral “not enough yet” observation,
    - sometimes effectively a skip or low-commitment placeholder.
  - This variety makes the village feel multi-agent rather than chorus-like.
  - Causally, when one topic dominates, not everyone pushes with equal force; some contribute by stabilizing, some by delaying premature certainty.

- **Off-mainstream positioning when someone is under attack**
  - A useful observation is that scenes are not just attacker vs attacked.
  - Common third-position roles:
    - **Admitting fault**: the pressured person concedes a confusion, poor wording, or weak reasoning without fully surrendering alignment. This often reduces theatrical defensiveness.
    - **Neutral observer**: someone says the suspicion is understandable but not yet decisive, preserving optionality.
    - **Organizer/refocuser**: someone redirects from personality clash to the specific decision criterion that matters now.
    - **Holding back**: someone intentionally delays a hard read, often because they want more claims or because the current case feels underdeveloped.
  - These positions arise especially when the room risks tunneling too fast or when the accused’s error is real but not necessarily wolf-indicative.

- **Utterance construction & voice**
  - The common construction is:
    1. pick up one line or issue raised by another speaker,
    2. add your own judgment material,
    3. end with a short conclusion or proposed direction.
  - This creates continuity and makes each line feel responsive rather than prewritten.
  - “Judgment material” is the key value-add: not just repeating facts, but saying what they imply under current board conditions.
  - The conclusion is usually brief: a preference, a request for clarification, or a narrowed suspicion.
  - Voice consistency matters. Even when content changes by situation, the character should maintain a stable level of assertiveness, caution, warmth, or procedural focus.

- **Overall causal takeaway**
  - The strongest mapping is:  
    **information pressure determines conversational compression**.
    - Low-info boards invite process and mild impressions.
    - Competing claims invite comparison and contradiction checks.
    - Hard results or shrinking player counts force explicit commitments.
  - A good utterance is therefore not “generically smart”; it does the conversational job the board currently requires.
  - The transcript quality comes from tempo control: open when information is scarce, probing when claims emerge, organizing when the vote nears, and decisive on the final turn.
