# Analysis — action (exemplar, from situations)

Personal memo on 5-player AIWolf action choices

This format is brutally small, so action choices are much more important than in larger villages. One wrong read often decides the game immediately or creates a forced endgame with almost no recovery. Because of that, the key observation is: actions should be driven less by “perfect information dreams” and more by preserving win-condition structure. In a 5-player game, every target choice changes the entire argument tree for the next day.

I’d organize my thinking by action type and by CO structure.

- General 5-player feel
  - The move budget is tiny. There is very little room for exploratory or “maybe this gives info later” play.
  - Day 1 is often the real game. Especially with 2-CO, the village is frequently deciding whether they can correctly identify the real seer immediately.
  - White side cannot spend villagers casually. A misvote plus a bad night kill often means collapse.
  - Because everyone knows this, claims and counterclaims tend to be sharper and more role-centered than in larger games.
  - So when choosing actions, ask: “What board state will exist tomorrow, and who will be forced into a narrower line?”

Divine (seer): in this situation, do this

- If there is 1-CO and you are the only seer claimant:
  - Divine an unconfirmed gray who is most likely to become the focal point of tomorrow’s vote.
  - In practice, this is often:
    - the player driving the discussion most strongly without being cleared,
    - the player whose reasoning feels slightly off but not enough to vote today,
    - or the quiet player who avoided commitment and may be hard to sort later.
  - Reason: in 1-CO, your result is expected to structure the next day. You want a target whose color will actually change the board, not a low-impact read.

- If there is 1-CO and one player is already broadly villager-like:
  - Do not spend the divine there just for comfort.
  - Instead, divine among the unresolved pair/trio who can realistically be executed.
  - In this setup, “useful result tomorrow” matters more than “confirming my hunch.”

- If there is 2-CO and you are the real seer:
  - Divine among the non-claimants, with priority on the player whose words best distinguish whether the fake seer is being supported by a wolf or by a mistaken villager.
  - In other words, pick the gray whose alignment will help untangle the seer-authenticity battle.
  - If one gray is aggressively backing the fake seer with weak logic, that target becomes attractive.
  - If one gray is low-content and likely to survive into final judgment, that target also becomes attractive.

- In 2-CO, do not divine the opposing seer claimant.
  - Their alignment is usually already strategically inferable: if they are not you, they are enemy-side.
  - The divine should solve a gray, not confirm what the board functionally knows.

- If one player made a key Day 0 / opening inconsistency:
  - Divine them when the inconsistency did not become executable today.
  - This is especially good when their tone lets them survive socially despite logical discomfort.

- If two grays exist and one is highly influential while the other is nearly silent:
  - Divine the one who is more likely to control the next vote, unless the silent one is becoming an “easy pocket” for wolf endgame.
  - Influence matters a lot in 5-player because one person can effectively swing the table.

Vote: in this situation, do this

- In 1-CO:
  - Default to trusting the solo seer result more strongly and pushing the process.
  - If the solo seer presents a black result on a gray, convergence should usually go there unless the claim behavior itself is deeply unnatural.
  - The village gains more by using the informational structure than by inventing elaborate anti-claim paranoia.
  - If there is no black result yet, votes tend to converge on the gray with the weakest consistency, poorest initiative, or most suspicious reaction to pressure.

- In 1-CO, if someone is trying to derail into “what if the solo seer is fake?” without solid grounds:
  - Treat that as suspicious unless there is a real contradiction.
  - The vote should stay process-oriented rather than drifting into abstract fear.

- In 2-CO:
  - This is essentially a seer-authenticity battle on Day 1.
  - Compare:
    - who claimed in more natural timing,
    - whose stated reasons are more internally consistent,
    - how each claimant’s read progression matches the conversation,
    - and how the non-claimants are aligning around them.
  - Day 0 / opening consistency matters a lot here because there is so little else to work with.
  - A claimant whose vote logic, suspicion order, or claim timing feels manufactured is a prime vote.

- In 2-CO, if one seer claimant gives reasoning that better anticipates board incentives:
  - Lean toward them being real.
  - Fake claims often sound “role-shaped” but not “board-shaped”; they say seer-like things without adapting well to this exact 5-player pressure.

- In 2-CO, if one claimant is supported mainly by vague tone-defense while the other has sharper logical grounding:
  - Vote the vaguer claimant.
  - In tiny games, concrete consistency usually deserves extra weight.

- If a non-claimer is behaving in a way that strongly implies coordination with a fake seer:
  - It can still be correct to vote the seer claimant first rather than the partner.
  - Removing the false claim often collapses the enemy narrative immediately.

Attack (werewolf): in this situation, do this

- If the real seer is apparent and broadly believed:
  - Attack the apparently-real seer.
  - In 5-player, letting a trusted real seer survive is often fatal because each extra result sharply compresses wolf options.

- If there is 1-CO and the solo seer is likely real:
  - Kill them unless there is a very specific reason to keep them alive as a future misvote target.
  - Usually, there isn’t enough room for fancy traps.

- If there is 2-CO and your possessed is the fake seer:
  - Often kill a strong villager instead of the rival seer if your possessed can still contest authenticity tomorrow.
  - This is especially attractive if the village is split and the fake claim remains viable.
  - You are preserving the battleground where your possessed can continue to absorb attention.

- If there is 2-CO and the rival seer is winning the authenticity battle:
  - Kill that rival seer.
  - Once the real seer becomes consensus-trusted, the possessed fake claim usually stops being useful.

- If one villager is highly influential, sorting people well, and likely to unify votes:
  - Attack that villager when the seer picture is already muddy enough.
  - Removing the organizer can be stronger than killing a less-trusted seer.

- If your possessed is in danger of being exposed by association:
  - Attack the player most likely to articulate that connection the next day.
  - In 5-player, deleting one persuasive voice can completely change how prior interactions are interpreted.

- With fewer players remaining:
  - Prioritize the kill that creates the simplest final-day misvote.
  - Do not think in terms of “best player to remove” abstractly; think in terms of “which survivor set gives me the most believable story tomorrow?”

Bottom line

- In this setup, actions should create the next day’s simplest favorable structure.
- Seer divines for maximum decision impact, not curiosity.
- Votes in 1-CO usually follow process; votes in 2-CO are mainly about authenticity comparison.
- Werewolf attacks either remove the trusted real seer or preserve a fake-claim battlefield, depending on which narrative is still alive.
- Because the village cannot afford many misses, consistency, claim timing, and who is shaping the table matter more than subtle long-term theory.
