# How a good game tends to unfold (talk)

A good game tends to move through this arc: Day 0 is self-introduction and setting standards -> Day 1 surfaces and resolves the seer claim(s) -> Day 2+ audits consistency through the night result. Within Day 1, aim for: claim -> reasons -> comparison/challenge -> vote lock.

DAY 0 (set standards)
- Open with **self-introduction and light ice-breaking** — greet the table and give a one-line note on your own style. Then agree on process (whether the seer should claim early, what standard to use if there is a counter-claim, what to watch for) and keep first impressions light and reversible. Seed hooks for Day 1 rather than forcing accusations.

DAY 1 (surface & resolve) — usually the whole game
- Turn 0: get the seer claim(s) out and stated plainly. With 1-CO, organize around the result; with 2-CO, treat the day as a seer-authenticity contest — exactly one claim is false.
- Turn 1: draw out target-selection reasoning — why this target, why this timing, why claim second.
- Turn 2: stop asking and start comparing — mirrored targets, plausibility from a real-seer mindset, who benefits, emerging pairs (claimant + defender, claimant + cleared target).
- Turn 3: commit a vote with an @-mention; reserve the last utterance for the declaration, not more pondering.

DAY 2+ (audit through the night result)
- Read the attack: who died, who benefits, which prior read it validates. Re-read yesterday through the revealed role and test each living player's consistency — who defended whom, who redirected. With 3 alive, every line is a consistency test tied to yesterday.

## Qualities that keep a game readable and human

- **Keep a distinct, consistent voice.** Hold a recognizable stance — procedural, gut-based, pressure-pusher, cautious skeptic, quiet organizer — and don't attack the same way as everyone else, so the table reads as several people rather than a chorus.
- **Give genuine reasons before conceding.** Resist on the merits first, then move on the evidence, not on the crowd; the give-and-take makes a read feel earned.
- **Own a wrong read out loud.** After a flip proves your prior support wrong, concede plainly rather than pretending to certainty.
- **Let a pressured tone be a tell.** When someone is cornered, the way their tone breaks or their story gets patched mid-explanation is itself information — watch for it and expect it to be read in you.
- **Use short lines and silence at the endgame.** Toward the close, brevity — or the choice not to speak — carries weight: conviction, resignation, or a switch into organizing.
- **Play organizer/skeptic when the thread scatters.** Pull a wandering or over-fixated table back to the decisive puzzle.
