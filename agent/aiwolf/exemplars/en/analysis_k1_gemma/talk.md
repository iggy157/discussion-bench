# Analysis — talk (exemplar, from scripts_k1)

Based on the provided exemplar, here are my observations regarding the causal mapping of the AIWolf discussion flow, intended as a reference for my future performance as a screenwriter-agent.

### 1. Board Situation $\rightarrow$ Agenda Mapping
The primary driver of the discussion is the "Information State" of the board. The agenda shifts based on the following variables:

*   **Seer CO (Come-Out) Distribution:**
    *   **1-CO:** The agenda is **Verification**. The village focuses on whether the claim is believable and if the result (Black/White) aligns with the target's behavior.
    *   **2-CO+:** The agenda shifts to **Conflict Resolution**. The priority is no longer the result itself, but the *meta-game* of the claims: Who claimed first? Who mirrored whom? Who is the "reactive" player?
*   **The "Black" (Werewolf) Result:**
    *   When a "Black" result is announced, the agenda becomes **Execution Validation**. The village must decide if the result is a genuine find or a "wolf-frame" (a wolf pretending to be a seer to eliminate a villager).
*   **Night-Attack Results:**
    *   **No Kill (Day 1):** Focuses entirely on the Seer's first-night result.
    *   **Kill Occurred (Day 2+):** The agenda starts with **Consistency Checking**. The village analyzes who benefited from the kill and if the dead player's previous behavior contradicts the current claims.
*   **Player Count/Distribution:**
    *   As the player count drops, the tempo accelerates. The "luxury" of neutral observation vanishes, and the agenda shifts from "getting a feel" to "hard binary choices" to avoid a werewolf victory by attrition.

### 2. Discussion Patterns & Situational Triggers
*   **The "Mirroring" Analysis:** Occurs specifically during **Multi-CO conflicts**. Instead of arguing about the target's guilt, players analyze the *timing* of the claims. (e.g., "Player B claimed only after Player A, using the same target—this is a reactive cover").
*   **The "Vibe vs. Logic" Tension:** Occurs during **Day 0 or early Day 1**. Some players establish a persona of "intuitive/tonal" reading, while others push for "procedural/logical" structure. This creates a social landscape where players can later pivot their arguments based on which "style" of evidence is currently winning.

### 3. Per-Day Phases
*   **Day 0 (The Calibration Phase):**
    *   **Goal:** Establish presence and social contracts.
    *   **Focus:** Self-introductions, agreeing on when Seers should claim, and establishing "baseline" behavior. No one is lynched; it is about mapping the "voice" of the table.
*   **Day 1 (The Revelation Phase):**
    *   **Goal:** Identify the first lead.
    *   **Focus:** Morning COs $\rightarrow$ Result sharing $\rightarrow$ Cross-examination of claims $\rightarrow$ Vote declaration. This is the highest-density information phase.
*   **Day 2+ (The Iteration Phase):**
    *   **Goal:** Narrow the suspect pool.
    *   **Focus:** Analyzing the night kill $\rightarrow$ Checking if the Seer's new result contradicts previous "White" claims $\rightarrow$ Identifying the "Possessed" player who may be shielding the Wolf.

### 4. Turn Progression & Closing
*   **Turn 0-2 (Deliberation):** The space for theory-crafting, questioning, and shifting the focus of suspicion.
*   **Final Turn (The Closing):** A strict transition from *discussion* to *decision*. The final utterance must be the vote declaration, using the `@Name` format. Deliberation ends here; the final turn is for commitment, not persuasion.

### 5. Spread of Responses (Avoiding Echo Chambers)
To maintain a natural, multi-agent feel, responses to a single agenda are distributed across different "stances" rather than unanimous agreement:
*   **The Driver:** Pushes the main theory (e.g., "Lynch Mara").
*   **The Skeptic:** Agrees with the logic but adds a caveat (e.g., "It makes sense, but the timing is still odd").
*   **The Outsider:** Offers a different angle or a delayed view (e.g., "I'm watching Felix more than Mara").
*   **The Neutral/Observer:** Refrains from a hard stance to gather more data (e.g., "I'll see how the claims hold up").
*   **The Passive:** Acknowledges and skips to save "social capital" for later.

### 6. Off-Mainstream Positioning (Under Attack)
When a character is targeted, they avoid a simple "I am innocent" binary. Depending on the situation, they adopt specific roles:
*   **The Logical Defender:** Points out the flaws in the accuser's logic (e.g., "You're picking me because I'm loud, not because I'm a wolf").
*   **The Counter-Accuser:** Flips the script to suggest the accuser is the actual wolf framing them.
*   **The Refocusing Organizer:** Attempts to move the conversation back to a larger board problem to dilute the heat on themselves.
*   **The Stoic:** Admits the suspicion is reasonable but maintains a calm, consistent narrative to appear "villager-like."

### 7. Utterance Construction & Voice
The "Good-Game" voice follows a specific additive structure:
**[Reference to previous speaker] $\rightarrow$ [New judgment/evidence] $\rightarrow$ [Concise conclusion].**

*   *Example:* "Felix likes procedure, I like that [Reference]. Owen's the watchful type [Observation]. Mara, you're the most forward—noted, not judged [Conclusion/Stance]."

This ensures the conversation feels like a cohesive thread rather than a series of isolated monologues. Each character maintains a consistent "persona" (e.g., the proceduralist, the intuitive, the cautious observer) throughout the game.
