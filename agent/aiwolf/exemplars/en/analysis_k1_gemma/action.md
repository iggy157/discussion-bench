### Observations on Exemplar Transcript

The provided transcript demonstrates a high-tempo, logically driven game where players prioritize "meta-game" positioning and behavioral analysis over simple guesswork. Several key atmospheric and structural elements stand out:

This creates a baseline of behavior that becomes evidence on Day 1. The agreement to share information early prevents the game from stagnating and forces the Werewolf/Possessed to commit to a strategy sooner.

The exemplar shows that the *timing* and *reasoning* behind a claim are more important than the claim itself. Felix’s mistake wasn't just claiming Seer, but claiming Seer *after* Tessa and choosing the *same target*. This "mirroring" is interpreted as a reactive move typical of a Possessed player trying to protect a teammate.

*   **Tempo and Flow:** The conversation moves rapidly from greeting $\rightarrow$ strategy $\rightarrow$ claim $\rightarrow$ cross-examination $\rightarrow$ verdict. There is a clear sense of urgency, reflecting the thin margins of a 5-player game.

### Personal Memo: Decision Mappings

#### Divine (Seer)
*   **In a "Quiet" Board:** If most players are passive, divine the one player who is attempting to steer the conversation or set the rules. They are the most likely to be a Wolf trying to control the flow.
*   **In a "Chaotic" Board:** If multiple people are arguing, divine the player who seems to be "agreeing" with everyone or playing the middle-man; they are often the Possessed trying to blend in.
*   **When unsure on Day 0:** Target the player who pushed for a specific "process" (like early COs). If they are a Wolf, their "helpful" structure is a mask.

#### Vote (Village/Seer/Possessed)
*   **Situation: Single Seer Claim (1-CO) with a "Werewolf" result:** Trust the result immediately. Push the village to execute the target to minimize the risk of a night kill.
*   **Situation: Double Seer Claim (2-CO) on the same target:**
    *   If the second claim is a "Human" result that mirrors the first claim's target $\rightarrow$ Vote the second claimant (likely Possessed) or the target (if the first claimant is more believable).
    *   Compare the "Why" of the check. If one player says "I checked them because they were loud" and the other says "I checked them because they mirrored the first Seer," the latter is often more logically consistent.
*   **Situation: Double Seer Claim (2-CO) on different targets:** Analyze Day 0 behavior. Vote the one whose claim contradicts their established persona or who seems to be reacting to the other's claim rather than leading with their own.

#### Attack (Werewolf)
*   **Situation: The real Seer has claimed and is believed:** Kill the Seer immediately. You cannot afford to let them divine the Possessed or another villager.
*   **Situation: The real Seer is doubted/contested (2-CO):** If the village is leaning toward the fake claim (Possessed), do NOT kill the real Seer yet. Let the village potentially execute the real Seer for you. Instead, kill the most influential "neutral" Villager who is casting doubt on your fake claim.
*   **Situation: Low player count (3-4 players):** Kill the player who is the "anchor" of the village's logic. If the village is fragmented, kill the one person who is successfully synthesizing the information.

#### 5-Player Specifics
*   **The "One-Mistake" Rule:** In a 5-player game, a single wrong vote on Day 1 usually leads to a loss. I must prioritize "certainty" over "hunches."
*   **The Seer-Authenticity Battle:** Since there is only one Wolf and one Possessed, a 2-CO situation is a binary fight. I must focus entirely on the *timing* of the claims. The first person to claim is usually the real Seer; the second is usually the "counter-claim."
*   **Resource Management:** The village has no room for error. If I am a villager, I must force the Seers to prove their logic quickly so a decision can be made before the next night phase.
