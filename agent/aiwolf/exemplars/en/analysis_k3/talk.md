# Observed flow of the reference games (talk)

In the reference scripts, the games generally follow this arc: Day 0 is self-introduction and setting standards -> Day 1 surfaces and resolves the seer claim(s) -> Day 2+ audits consistency through the night result. Within Day 1 the turns run: claim -> reasons -> comparison/challenge -> vote lock. Concretely, they tend to unfold as follows.

DAY 0 (set standards)
- Day 0 opens with **self-introductions and light ice-breaking** — each player greets the table and gives a one-line note on their own style. Only then does the table agree on process (whether the seer should claim early, what standard to use if there is a counter-claim, what each player will watch for) and trade light, reversible first impressions. No hard accusations — the point is to warm up and seed hooks for Day 1.

DAY 1 (surface & resolve) — usually the whole game
- Turn 0: the seer(s) are prompted to claim; results are stated plainly.
  - 1-CO: the table organizes around the result unless it is wildly inconsistent with prior behavior.
  - 2-CO (the most common focal pattern): the day collapses into a seer-authenticity contest — exactly one claim is false.
- Turn 1: target-selection reasoning is drawn out — why this target, why this timing, why claim second.
- Turn 2: the group stops asking and starts comparing — mirrored targets, whether a choice is plausible from a real-seer mindset, who benefits, and emerging pairs (claimant + defender, claimant + cleared target).
- Turn 3: everyone commits a vote with an @-mention; the final utterance is reserved for the declaration, not more pondering.

DAY 2+ (audit through the night result)
- Open by reading the attack: who died, who benefits, which prior read it validates.
- Re-read yesterday through the revealed role (fake flips possessed vs wolf; real seer dies) and test each living player's consistency — who defended whom, who redirected.
- With 3 alive there is no room for flourish: every line is a consistency test tied to yesterday.

This is how the reference scripts use the game's days and turns.

## What makes these scripts readable and human

Beyond the day/turn arc, the scripts stay engaging because of *how* the characters talk, not just what they conclude:

- **Distinct, consistent character voices.** Each keeps a recognizable stance — procedural, gut-based, pressure-pusher, cautious skeptic, quiet organizer — and not everyone attacks the same way, so the table reads as several people rather than a chorus.
- **Genuine reasons before conceding.** Characters resist on the merits first and then move on the evidence, not on the crowd; the give-and-take is what makes a read feel earned.
- **Owning a wrong read out loud.** After a flip proves prior support wrong, a character concedes plainly — which reads more human than overpolished certainty.
- **Tells in how a pressured player's tone shifts.** When someone is cornered, the way their tone breaks or their story gets patched mid-explanation is itself information the table reads.
- **Short lines and silence at the endgame.** Toward the close, brevity — or the choice not to speak — carries weight: conviction, resignation, or a switch into organizing.
- **An organizer/skeptic who re-centers the seer war.** When the thread scatters or over-fixates on one target, someone pulls it back to the decisive puzzle.

Carry these qualities over — a consistent voice, real pushback, honest updates, and functional variety across the table — so the game reads like a real table under pressure, not four identical accusers.
